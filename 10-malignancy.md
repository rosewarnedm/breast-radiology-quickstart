Breast cancer detection and characterisation underpin much of breast radiology practice. This chapter reviews DCIS, invasive carcinoma, staging principles and special presentations, integrating mammography, DBT, ultrasound, MRI and CEM with image-guided biopsy and multidisciplinary management.

{% tab chapter-10 DCIS %}

## Overview

DCIS is a non-obligate precursor to invasive carcinoma characterised by malignant epithelial proliferation confined within the basement membrane. It accounts for ~20–25% of screen-detected cancers and is usually identified by suspicious microcalcifications.

## Mammography and DBT

- Calcifications are the dominant imaging feature; mass or architectural distortion (AD) is uncommon and often indicates invasive component.
- **Morphology** (in increasing concern):
  - Amorphous — low–intermediate suspicion
  - Coarse heterogeneous — intermediate
  - **Fine pleomorphic** — suspicious
  - **Fine linear or fine-linear branching** — highly suspicious for high-grade DCIS
- **Distribution** (in increasing concern):
  - Regional / diffuse — usually benign
  - Grouped — intermediate
  - **Linear or segmental** — suspicious for ductal disease

## Ultrasound and MRI

- Ultrasound often negative; may show ductal dilatation, hypoechoic non-mass area, or echogenic foci within a duct.
- MRI typically demonstrates **non-mass enhancement (NME)** with:
  - **Segmental or linear distribution**
  - **Clumped or clustered-ring internal pattern**
- DWI may show restricted diffusion but is less reliable than for invasive disease.

## Biopsy and Localisation

- <<Stereotactic or DBT-guided vacuum-assisted biopsy (VAB, 7–10G) is the technique of choice for suspicious microcalcifications>>, reducing sampling error vs 14G core.
- <<Specimen radiography must confirm representative calcifications have been sampled>>.
- Place a marker clip after biopsy.
- For extensive calcifications, bracket localisation is often needed pre-surgery.

## Reporting Essentials

- Measure **maximal calcification span** (often exceeds eventual invasive size).
- Document separate groups and distribution.
- Note any associated mass, AD or NME — these raise suspicion for occult invasion.
- Communicate that imaging cannot exclude microinvasion — final assessment is histopathological.

## Pitfalls

- Benign calcifications mimicking DCIS: skin (tangential views), vascular (tram-track), milk of calcium (layering on lateral views), fat necrosis (rim/eggshell).
- DCIS extent may be **underestimated by mammography** if low-grade and non-calcifying — consider MRI when extent is critical.
- Paget disease may have underlying DCIS without mammographic findings — image and biopsy the nipple–areolar complex.

{% tab chapter-10 Invasive Carcinoma %}

## Histological Spectrum

- **Invasive carcinoma of no special type (NST/ductal)** — most common (~70–80%).
- **Invasive lobular carcinoma (ILC)** — diffuse, single-file infiltration; often subtle.
- Special types: tubular, mucinous, medullary-like, papillary, metaplastic.
- Receptor subtypes (ER/PR/HER2) influence imaging phenotype:
  - **Triple-negative**: often round/oval, circumscribed, rapidly growing — can mimic benign masses.
  - **Luminal A**: more typically spiculated/irregular.
  - **HER2-enriched**: associated with extensive DCIS and pleomorphic calcifications.

## Mammography / DBT

Malignant mass features:
- **Shape**: irregular (suspicious); round/oval can occur in TNBC.
- **Margins** (increasing concern): indistinct → microlobulated → **spiculated**.
- **Density**: high relative to fibroglandular tissue is suspicious.
- **Architectural distortion** with or without a central mass — suspicious for invasive carcinoma or radial scar; ILC frequently presents this way.
- Associated findings: skin/nipple retraction, skin thickening, trabecular thickening.

## Ultrasound

Suspicious B-mode features:
- **Shape**: irregular.
- **Orientation**: **non-parallel (taller-than-wide)** is suspicious; parallel does not exclude malignancy.
- **Margins**: spiculated, angular, microlobulated or indistinct.
- **Echotexture**: hypoechoic; heterogeneous; posterior acoustic shadowing common.
- Associated: echogenic halo, duct extension, skin/oedema changes, axillary adenopathy.
- Elastography: malignant lesions are usually stiff; <<shear-wave values >80–100 kPa raise suspicion but thresholds are vendor- and unit-specific — use as adjunct only>>.

## MRI

- Mass: irregular shape, spiculated/irregular margins, heterogeneous or **rim enhancement**.
- **Kinetic curves**:
  - **Type I (persistent) — usually benign**
  - **Type II (plateau) — intermediate**
  - **Type III (washout) — suspicious**
- DWI: malignant lesions typically restrict; <<reported ADC for invasive cancer is often <1.0–1.3 × 10⁻³ mm²/s, though protocol-dependent — always interpret with morphology>>.
- NME with segmental/linear distribution and clumped/clustered-ring pattern: suspicious, common in DCIS and ILC.

## Contrast-Enhanced Mammography (CEM)

- Dual-energy technique highlighting tumour neoangiogenesis.
- Useful for extent in dense breasts and as MRI alternative when contraindicated.
- <<Radiation dose approximately 20–40% higher than 2D mammography but within regulatory limits>>.
- Contraindicated in pregnancy and severe iodinated contrast allergy.

## Special Presentations

| Entity | Key features |
|---|---|
| **Inflammatory breast cancer (T4d)** | Rapid (≤6 months) erythema/oedema/peau d'orange involving ≥1/3 breast; skin thickening; dermal lymphatic invasion on biopsy |
| **Paget disease** | Eczematous nipple change; may have underlying DCIS or invasive cancer; image with mammography/US/MRI |
| **ILC** | Subtle mammography; AD without mass; MRI essential for extent |
| **Male breast cancer** | Eccentric subareolar mass ± nipple retraction; distinguish from gynaecomastia (flame-shaped, central, often bilateral) |
| **BIA-ALCL** | Late (>1 year) peri-implant effusion/mass; textured implants; US-guided aspiration with CD30 IHC |
| **Pregnancy-associated** | US first-line; mammography with shielding if needed; avoid gadolinium |

## Biopsy Principles

- **US-guided 14G core** for sonographically visible masses and lymph nodes — first-line.
- **Stereotactic / DBT-guided VAB** for calcifications and tomosynthesis-only distortions.
- **MRI-guided biopsy** for MRI-only lesions.
- <<Imaging–pathology discordance (benign result in BI-RADS 4/5 lesion) mandates repeat biopsy or surgical excision after MDT discussion — never accept discordance>>.
- Place a marker clip after every percutaneous biopsy.

## BI-RADS Assessment Categories

| Category | Meaning | Likelihood of malignancy | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | Incomplete | — | Additional imaging |
| 1 | Negative | ~0% | Routine |
| 2 | Benign | ~0% | Routine |
| 3 | Probably benign | <<≤2%>> | <<Short-interval (6-month) follow-up>> |
| 4A | Low suspicion | <<2–10%>> | Biopsy |
| 4B | Moderate suspicion | <<10–50%>> | Biopsy |
| 4C | High suspicion | <<50–95%>> | Biopsy |
| 5 | Highly suggestive | <<≥95%>> | Appropriate action |
| 6 | Known malignancy | 100% | As clinically indicated |

## AJCC 8th Edition T Staging

- **Tis**: in situ (DCIS, Paget without invasion)
- **T1mi**: <<microinvasion ≤1 mm>>
- **T1a**: >1–5 mm
- **T1b**: >5–10 mm
- **T1c**: >10–20 mm
- **T2**: >20–50 mm
- **T3**: >50 mm
- **T4**:
  - **T4a**: chest wall extension (excluding pectoralis only)
  - **T4b**: skin oedema/ulceration/satellite nodules (including peau d'orange)
  - **T4c**: T4a + T4b
  - **T4d**: <<inflammatory carcinoma — clinical diagnosis with ≥1/3 breast skin involvement of ≤6 months duration>>

## Clinical N Staging

- **cN0**: no regional nodes.
- **cN1**: mobile ipsilateral level I/II axillary nodes.
- **cN2**: fixed/matted axillary nodes, or internal mammary nodes without axillary disease.
- **cN3**: infraclavicular, supraclavicular, or combination with internal mammary disease.

## Axillary Ultrasound

Suspicious features (morphology trumps size):
- <<Diffuse cortical thickness ≥3 mm or focal eccentric cortical bulge>>
- Loss / replacement of fatty hilum
- Round shape (increased short-to-long axis ratio)
- Non-hilar cortical vascularity
- Short-axis size >7–8 mm raises concern (variable)

Management:
- Sample suspicious nodes (core preferred over FNA).
- <<Place a clip in any biopsied positive node when neoadjuvant therapy is planned, to enable targeted axillary dissection (TAD)>>.

## Multifocal vs Multicentric Disease

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Multifocal** | Multiple foci in the **same quadrant** (typically within 4–5 cm) |
| **Multicentric** | Foci in **different quadrants** or >5 cm apart |
| **Bilateral** | Synchronous contralateral disease (~2–4% at diagnosis) |

- MRI is the most sensitive modality for multifocality/multicentricity, especially in ILC and dense breasts.
- Surgical implications: multicentric disease usually precludes breast-conserving surgery.

## MRI Indications for Staging

- Newly diagnosed ILC.
- Dense breasts with high suspicion of underestimation.
- Discordant mammography/US findings.
- Occult primary with axillary metastasis.
- Genetic predisposition (BRCA1/2 etc.).
- Pre-NAST baseline.
- Suspected chest wall or skin involvement.

## Neoadjuvant Systemic Therapy (NAST): Imaging Role

**Before NAST**:
- Baseline measurements on the most accurate modality (often MRI).
- <<Place marker clip(s) in the primary tumour AND in any biopsied positive axillary node>> — essential for post-treatment localisation and TAD.
- Document multifocality, skin/nipple involvement, axillary burden.

**During/after NAST**:
- Ultrasound for interval response and nodal reassessment.
- <<MRI is the most accurate modality for residual disease and pCR prediction; suspicious residual patterns include enhancing mass or clumped NME>>.
- DWI/ADC: increasing ADC suggests response.
- Imaging cannot guarantee pathological complete response — communicate residual uncertainty.

## Reporting Checklist

1. Indication, side, technique, density.
2. Lesion location (clock-face, distance from nipple), 3D size.
3. Morphology in BI-RADS lexicon.
4. Associated calcifications, AD, NME.
5. Distances to skin, nipple, pectoral fascia / chest wall.
6. Multifocal / multicentric / bilateral status.
7. Axillary and regional nodal assessment (levels, morphology, size).
8. Comparison with prior imaging.
9. Final BI-RADS / R category with clear management recommendation.
10. Post-procedure: clip location, specimen radiography findings.

## Contraindications and Safety

- <<MRI gadolinium contraindicated in pregnancy; in severe renal impairment follow local GBCA policy>>.
- <<CEM contraindicated in pregnancy and severe iodinated contrast allergy>>.
- Mammography during pregnancy: avoid unless benefit outweighs risk; foetal dose is low with shielding.
- Biopsy: address coagulopathy and avoid infected entry sites.

## Key Take-Aways

- DCIS most commonly presents as **fine pleomorphic or fine linear/branching calcifications in a segmental or linear distribution**; <<stereotactic/DBT-guided VAB with specimen radiography is the biopsy method of choice>>.
- Invasive cancer suspicion rests on **irregular shape, spiculated margins, non-parallel orientation on US, type III kinetics and restricted diffusion on MRI** — though triple-negative cancers can mimic benign circumscribed masses.
- <<BI-RADS thresholds: category 3 ≤2%, 4A 2–10%, 4B 10–50%, 4C 50–95%, 5 ≥95%>> — use these consistently to drive management.
- Axillary node assessment relies on **cortical morphology, not size alone**; <<clip biopsy-positive nodes before NAST to enable targeted axillary dissection>>.
- **MRI is the most accurate modality** for ILC extent, multifocality/multicentricity and NAST response assessment, with key indications including dense breasts, occult primary and discordant findings.
- **Never accept imaging–pathology discordance** in a BI-RADS 4/5 lesion — repeat biopsy or proceed to surgical excision after MDT discussion.
