This chapter covers contrast-enhanced mammography (CEM) alongside the core interventional techniques used in breast radiology practice. It focuses on when to use each technique, how to perform it safely, and how to integrate the result into multidisciplinary patient care.

{% tab chapter-5 Contrast-Enhanced Mammography %}

## Clinical Context and Indications

CEM combines iodinated intravenous contrast with dual-energy mammography to display contrast uptake (neoangiogenesis) alongside morphology. It is increasingly used as a rapid, accessible alternative to MRI in several scenarios.

**Common indications:**
- Problem-solving for indeterminate mammographic/ultrasound findings (BI-RADS 0/3 with persistent concern).
- Staging of newly diagnosed breast cancer to assess extent, multifocality/multicentricity, and contralateral disease.
- Assessment of response to neoadjuvant systemic therapy (NAST).
- Evaluation of suspected recurrence in a treated breast, especially in dense parenchyma or post-surgical scarring.
- Screening in selected higher-than-population-risk women or those with dense breasts where MRI is contraindicated, unavailable, or not tolerated.
- Pre-operative mapping when there is discordance between ultrasound/mammography and clinical findings.

**Relative/less common indications:**
- Occult primary presenting with axillary metastasis (if MRI is unavailable or contraindicated).
- Problem-solving for calcifications suspicious for DCIS (note enhancement is variable in low-grade DCIS).

## Contraindications and Precautions

**Absolute:**
- <<Previous severe immediate hypersensitivity reaction to iodinated contrast is an absolute contraindication unless under specialist premedication and risk–benefit discussion>>.
- Unstable asthma/active anaphylaxis.

**Relative:**
- <<Renal impairment: proceed if eGFR ≥30 mL/min/1.73 m²; if eGFR <30 or AKI, consider alternatives>>.
- <<Metformin: if eGFR ≥30 continue; if eGFR <30 or AKI, withhold at time of contrast and recheck renal function at 48 hours before restarting (ESUR guidance)>>.
- Pregnancy: generally avoid unless benefits outweigh risks.
- <<Breastfeeding is safe to continue after iodinated contrast; interruption is not required>>.
- Unstable hyperthyroidism: consult endocrinology.

## Technical Principles

- Dual-energy technique acquires low-energy (LE) images equivalent to standard mammography and high-energy (HE) images <<above the iodine K-edge of 33.2 keV>>.
- Proprietary recombination creates a subtracted "contrast" image showing iodine uptake.
- LE images are used for standard BI-RADS mammographic assessment.
- Recombined images display enhancement, analogous to MRI early post-contrast imaging.
- CEM does not routinely provide kinetic curves; interpretation is morphological plus relative enhancement intensity/pattern.

**Radiation dose:** <<Average glandular dose (AGD) for CEM is typically 1.2–1.5 times that of standard FFDM for the same projections>>.

## Patient Preparation and Safety

- Screening: prior iodinated contrast reaction, asthma/atopy, renal function (eGFR within 3 months if risk factors), metformin, pregnancy.
- Resuscitation facilities and anaphylaxis kit must be immediately available.
- IV access: 18–22G cannula in antecubital fossa or forearm.

**Contrast protocol (typical):**
- <<Non-ionic iodinated contrast, dose 1.0–1.5 mL/kg, injection rate 2–3 mL/s, followed by 20–30 mL saline flush>>.
- <<Begin acquisitions at approximately 2 minutes post-injection; complete bilateral CC and MLO views within 7 minutes>>.

**Extravasation:** stop infusion, elevate limb, apply cold compress, document and monitor.

## Acquisition: Step-by-Step

1. Check identity, side, indication, renal/allergy screen; obtain consent.
2. Insert IV cannula; confirm patency.
3. Position patient for first view (typically MLO of the more suspicious side first).
4. Inject contrast at 2–3 mL/s with saline flush.
5. Acquire sequence within ~7 minutes: MLO affected → MLO contralateral → CC affected → CC contralateral.
6. Maintain consistent, firm compression to minimise motion and dose.
7. Review for motion; repeat if necessary within enhancement window.

**Image quality tips:**
- Adequate compression reduces motion and improves subtraction.
- Avoid motion between LE/HE pairs (misregistration artefacts).
- Remove non-clinical metal/skin markers that may cause subtraction artefacts.

## Interpretation Approach

**Read LE images first** using standard mammographic BI-RADS descriptors (mass shape/margin/density, calcification morphology/distribution, distortion).

**Then read recombined images:**
- Background parenchymal enhancement (BPE): minimal, mild, moderate, marked.
- Lesion enhancement: focus (<5 mm), mass, or non-mass enhancement (NME).
- Mass internal enhancement: homogeneous, heterogeneous, **rim (suspicious)**, septations.
- NME distribution: focal, linear, **segmental (suspicious for DCIS)**, regional, diffuse.
- Relative intensity: none, mild, moderate, marked (qualitative).

**Final BI-RADS assessment** integrates LE, enhancement, and ultrasound:
- <<BI-RADS 3 ≤2% malignancy risk; BI-RADS 5 ≥95% risk>>.

**Suspicious patterns:** irregular/spiculated mass with moderate–marked enhancement; segmental/linear NME; rim enhancement; enhancing architectural distortion.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Misregistration/motion:** rim or "tramline" artefact at edges.
- **High BPE** (premenopausal, luteal phase, HRT): <<schedule CEM on days 7–14 of the menstrual cycle and consider withholding HRT 4–6 weeks before imaging if feasible>>.
- **Vascular enhancement** mimicking linear NME: trace continuity on LE.
- **Post-biopsy/radiotherapy inflammation**: document interval from intervention.
- <<Low-grade DCIS may not enhance on CEM; do not downstage on absence of enhancement—LE morphology governs>>.
- **Skin/nipple enhancement**: normal; avoid overcalling.
- **Implants:** CEM possible with Eklund views but does not assess implant integrity.

## Where CEM Fits vs MRI

- CEM has sensitivity approaching MRI for invasive cancer and high-grade DCIS, with shorter exam time and lower cost.
- **MRI remains preferred for:** implant integrity, chest wall involvement, detailed kinetic assessment, highest-risk screening (e.g., BRCA), and MRI-only lesions needing MRI-guided biopsy.

## General Principles and Safety

**Indications:** sampling BI-RADS 4–5 lesions; selected BI-RADS 3 with risk factors; nodal staging; preoperative work-up.

**Modality choice:**
- Ultrasound guidance first-line if lesion visible.
- Stereotactic/tomosynthesis for calcifications/distortion not seen on ultrasound.
- MRI-guided biopsy for MRI-only lesions.

**Consent:** discuss risks (pain, bruising/haematoma, infection <1%, vasovagal syncope, rare pneumothorax, clip migration, need for further procedures).

**Anticoagulation/haemostasis thresholds (local policy varies):**
- <<Platelets: proceed if >50 × 10⁹/L (prefer >75 × 10⁹/L for VAB/VAE)>>.
- <<Warfarin: hold to INR <1.5 before core biopsy or VAB>>.
- <<DOACs: omit 24 hours pre-procedure if CrCl ≥50 mL/min; 48 hours if renal impairment; restart 24 hours post-procedure if haemostasis secure>>.
- Aspirin: usually continue.
- Clopidogrel: consider holding 5–7 days for VAB/VAE if safe.

**Local anaesthetic:** 1% lidocaine, typical total dose 5–10 mL; aspirate before injection.

## Ultrasound-Guided Core Needle Biopsy: Step-by-Step

**Equipment:** 14G automated spring-loaded core device; coaxial introducer optional; linear high-frequency probe; sterile sheath; clip device.

1. Position patient comfortably with arm abducted; stabilise.
2. Identify lesion in two planes; optimise depth and gain; use Doppler to avoid vessels.
3. Plan **in-plane approach** along the long axis of the lesion; mark skin.
4. Skin prep and drape; infiltrate LA to skin and along the tract to the deep margin of the lesion.
5. Small skin nick with 11 blade if needed.
6. Advance needle under **continuous visualisation**; keep echogenic tip in-plane.
7. Fire device when sampling notch traverses the target; <<obtain 3–5 cores for most solid masses>>.
8. For US-visible calcifications: obtain specimen radiograph to confirm calcifications in cores.
9. Deploy a marker clip when appropriate (especially before NAST, for calcifications, or near-complete removal).
10. Haemostasis: firm compression 10–15 minutes; steri-strips and dressing.
11. Counsel: red flags (increasing pain/swelling, fever, discharge), contact details.

**Tips and pitfalls:**
- Always see the needle tip — out-of-plane passes are the commonest cause of misses.
- Hydrodissection and "heel-toe" create safe windows for superficial/chest wall lesions.
- For very superficial targets, use a shallow angle or a skin-protection device.
- For deep/chest wall lesions, keep the needle **parallel to chest wall** to minimise pneumothorax risk.

## Stereotactic/Tomosynthesis-Guided Core Biopsy

Options: upright add-on, prone table, or tomosynthesis-guided systems.

1. Position and compress with lesion centred in the window.
2. Acquire stereotactic pair (e.g., ±15°) or tomosynthesis sweep; calculate X, Y, Z coordinates.
3. LA and small skin nick; advance needle to calculated Z-depth; confirm with pre-fire image.
4. Fire device; <<obtain at least 6 cores when performing stereotactic core biopsy for calcifications>>.
5. Verify with specimen radiography for calcifications.
6. Place clip; confirm position with two-view mammography.

**Pitfalls:**
- Thin compressed breast increases risk of skin breach — adjust approach or reduce compression slightly once needle is in.
- Target shift with motion — coach patient and support breast.
- For distortion, target the centre of the distortion on the tomosynthesis slice where most prominent.

## MRI-Guided Biopsy (Outline)

- **Indication:** MRI-only enhancing lesion without ultrasound or mammographic correlate.
- Requires dedicated breast coil, targeting grid, non-ferromagnetic needles, post-contrast imaging.
- Steps: pre-contrast localisation → contrast → identify target → sample (usually VAB) → MRI-safe clip → confirmation series.

## FNA and Cyst Aspiration

- **FNA:** 21–23G needle, 10 mL syringe.
- Useful for cyst aspiration (symptomatic relief; send cytology if haemorrhagic/atypical) and axillary node cytology where core is not feasible.
- Limitations: lower sensitivity/specificity vs core; no architectural assessment; not preferred for solid masses.

## Axillary Interventions

**Suspicious ultrasound features:**
- <<Cortical thickness >3 mm (focal or diffuse)>>.
- Focal cortical bulge or eccentric cortical thickening.
- Loss/replacement of fatty hilum.
- Round shape and non-hilar cortical vascularity.

**Technique:**
- Core biopsy (14G) preferred for histology; FNA acceptable if core is risky.
- **Clip the biopsied positive node** to facilitate targeted retrieval after NAST (TAD).
- For TAD: place seed/tag into clipped node preoperatively; combined with SLN biopsy improves staging.

## Complications and Management

- **Haematoma:** compression, cold packs 24–48 hours, analgesia.
- **Infection (<1%):** oral antibiotics for cellulitis; abscess drainage.
- **Vasovagal syncope:** Trendelenburg, oxygen, airway support.
- **Pneumothorax:** extremely rare; suspect with chest pain/dyspnoea; CXR if clinically indicated.
- **Seed/wire migration:** minimise manipulation; document and inform surgeon promptly.

## VAB and VAE: Indications and Technique

**Indications:**
- Sampling architectural distortion or calcifications (especially stereotactic/tomosynthesis-guided).
- Lesions requiring larger volume sampling.
- B3 lesions for upgrade assessment.
- Symptomatic benign lesions (e.g., fibroadenoma) for excision (VAE).

**Needles:** <<typically 7–10G for VAE and 9–12G for VAB>>.

**Technique (US- or stereo-guided):**
1. Plan trajectory; LA; small skin incision.
2. Advance probe; confirm aperture orientation facing lesion.
3. Acquire sequential samples circumferentially by rotating the device.
4. For calcifications: perform specimen radiography; continue sampling until calcifications are cleared or significantly reduced in the target zone.
5. **Clip placement is mandatory after VAB/VAE.**
6. Compression 15–20 minutes; pressure dressing; consider elastic binder if large cavity.

**Complications:** haematoma (apply prolonged compression; tranexamic-soaked gauze per local practice), skin tethering if too superficial, vasovagal events. Avoid when anticoagulation cannot be safely managed.

## CEM-Guided Biopsy/Localisation (Emerging)

- Some platforms enable CEM-guided biopsy or localisation for CEM-only enhancing lesions without US/mammo correlates.
- Consider referral to centres with capability if the finding is actionable and other modalities cannot target it.

## Preoperative Localisation Techniques

| Technique | Key features | Considerations |
|---|---|---|
| **Wire-guided (WGL)** | US/stereo placement; wire tip 5–10 mm beyond lesion | Same-day placement preferred; avoid trans-nipple route |
| **¹²⁵I seed (RSL)** | Days–weeks pre-op; gamma probe detection | Strict radiation governance and chain-of-custody |
| **Magnetic seed (Magseed)** | Non-radioactive; magnetometer detector | <<Avoid placement <10 mm from skin to limit interference and palpability>> |
| **Radar reflector (SAVI SCOUT)** | Non-radioactive; radar console | Flexible scheduling; MRI-compatible considerations |
| **RF tag (LOCalizer)** | Non-radioactive; RFID detector | Multiple targets possible |
| **ROLL** | ⁹⁹ᵐTc tracer injected; gamma probe | Same-day procedure typical |
| **Carbon track** | Charcoal tattoo of tract | Inexpensive; risk of skin staining |

**Documentation:** lesion, approach, device type/lot, final marker position relative to lesion in orthogonal planes, complications, and post-localisation images.

## Clip Placement and Verification

- Place a clip after any biopsy likely to alter lesion appearance (tumours before NAST, calcifications, VAB/VAE).
- Avoid deploying into a haematoma (migration risk).
- Post-deployment: **two-view mammogram** to confirm position; document distance from nipple and quadrant.

## Specimen Handling and Radiography

- For calcifications: perform specimen radiography on cores/VAB fragments; indicate presence and density of calcification; send radiograph with pathology request.
- For VAE of benign lesions: label as "excision" and orient if required.
- Communicate target and radiology suspicion to pathology — **radiology–pathology correlation is critical**.

## Radiology–Pathology Correlation: UK B-Categorisation

| Category | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **B1** | Normal tissue or inadequate sample |
| **B2** | Benign |
| **B3** | Lesion of uncertain malignant potential (ADH, LN, RS/CSL, papillary, mucocoele-like) |
| **B4** | Suspicious |
| **B5a** | Malignant — in situ |
| **B5b** | Malignant — invasive |

**Concordance assessment:**
- **Concordant benign:** imaging and pathology agree → routine/symptomatic follow-up.
- **Discordant benign:** imaging suspicious but benign histology → repeat biopsy (consider VAB), alternative modality, or excision.
- **B3 lesions:** usually require VAB/VAE for upgrade assessment or diagnostic excision; tailored to subtype.
- **Malignant:** ensure receptor testing, clip in situ, plan surgery/systemic therapy; consider further staging (CEM/MRI).

## Post-Procedure Aftercare and Patient Advice

- Keep dressing dry for 24–48 hours; remove after 48 hours if dry.
- Expect bruising/tenderness for up to 1–2 weeks.
- **Red flags:** rapidly enlarging swelling, severe uncontrolled pain, fever, purulent discharge, shortness of breath — urgent review.
- Activity: avoid heavy lifting with affected arm 24–48 hours; supportive bra recommended.
- Anticoagulants: restart per plan once haemostasis secure (typically 24 hours post-procedure).

## Key Take-Aways

- CEM uses dual-energy mammography after iodinated contrast to combine morphological assessment (LE images) with neoangiogenesis-driven enhancement (recombined images), giving sensitivity approaching MRI at lower cost and shorter exam time.
- Optimise CEM technique with 1.0–1.5 mL/kg contrast at 2–3 mL/s, imaging from 2 minutes and completing within 7 minutes; schedule premenopausal patients on cycle days 7–14 to limit BPE.
- Ultrasound-guided 14G core biopsy with 3–5 cores is first-line for visible lesions; use stereotactic/tomosynthesis guidance for calcifications and distortion, and MRI-guided biopsy for MRI-only findings.
- Place a marker clip after any biopsy likely to alter lesion appearance (pre-NAST tumours, calcifications, VAB/VAE) and confirm position with two-view mammography.
- Always assess radiology–pathology concordance; B3 lesions usually warrant VAB/VAE or excision, and discordant benign results require repeat sampling or excision.
- Suspicious axillary nodes (cortical thickness >3 mm) should be sampled with ultrasound-guided core biopsy before surgery.
