This chapter covers contrast-enhanced mammography (CEM) and the core interventional techniques used in breast radiology. It focuses on practical steps, safety, image interpretation, and multidisciplinary decision-making. For each technique, know when to use it, how to perform it safely, and how to integrate the result into patient care.

{% tab chapter-5 Contrast-Enhanced Mammography %}

## Clinical Context and Indications

CEM combines iodinated intravenous contrast with dual-energy mammography to display contrast uptake (neoangiogenesis) alongside morphology. It is increasingly used as a rapid, accessible alternative to MRI in several scenarios.

**Common indications:**
- Problem-solving for indeterminate mammographic/ultrasound findings (BI-RADS 0/3 with persistent concern).
- Staging of newly diagnosed breast cancer to assess extent, multifocality/multicentricity, and contralateral disease.
- Assessment of response to neoadjuvant systemic therapy (NAST).
- Evaluation of suspected recurrence in a treated breast (especially in dense parenchyma or post-surgical scarring).
- Screening in selected higher-than-population-risk women or those with dense breasts where MRI is contraindicated, unavailable, or not tolerated.
- Pre-operative mapping when there is discordance between ultrasound/mammography and clinical findings.

**Relative/less common indications:**
- Occult primary presenting with axillary metastasis (if MRI is unavailable or contraindicated).
- Problem-solving for calcifications suspicious for DCIS (note that enhancement is variable in low-grade DCIS).

## Contraindications and Precautions

**Absolute:**
- <<Previous severe immediate hypersensitivity reaction to iodinated contrast is an absolute contraindication unless specialist premedication and risk–benefit discussion are undertaken.>>
- Unstable asthma/active anaphylaxis.

**Relative:**
- <<Renal impairment: proceed if eGFR ≥30 mL/min/1.73 m²; if eGFR <30 or AKI, consider alternatives or mitigate risk with hydration.>>
- <<Metformin: continue if eGFR ≥30; if eGFR <30 or AKI, withhold at time of contrast and recheck renal function at 48 hours before restarting (ESUR guidance).>>
- Pregnancy: generally avoid unless benefits outweigh risks.
- <<Breastfeeding: safe to continue; interruption is not required after iodinated contrast.>>
- Unstable hyperthyroidism: consult endocrinology.

## Technical Principles

- Dual-energy technique acquires low-energy (LE) images equivalent to standard mammography and high-energy (HE) images <<above the iodine K-edge at 33.2 keV>>.
- Proprietary recombination creates a subtracted "contrast" image showing iodine uptake.
- LE images are used for standard BI-RADS mammographic assessment.
- Recombined images display enhancement (surrogate of tumour angiogenesis), analogous to MRI early post-contrast imaging.
- CEM does not routinely provide kinetic curves; interpretation is morphological plus relative enhancement.

**Radiation dose:**
- <<Average glandular dose for CEM is typically 1.2–1.5 times that of standard FFDM for the same projections.>>

## Patient Preparation and Safety

- Screening questions: prior iodinated contrast reaction, asthma/atopy, renal function, metformin, pregnancy.
- Resuscitation facilities and an anaphylaxis kit must be immediately available.
- IV access: 18–22G cannula in antecubital fossa or forearm.

**Contrast protocol (typical):**
- <<Non-ionic iodinated contrast at 1.0–1.5 mL/kg, injection rate 2–3 mL/s, followed by 20–30 mL saline flush.>>
- <<Begin acquisitions at approximately 2 minutes post-injection and complete bilateral CC and MLO views within 7 minutes.>>

## Acquisition: Step-by-Step

1. Check identity, side, indication, renal/allergy screen; obtain consent.
2. Insert IV cannula; confirm patency.
3. Position patient for first view (typically MLO of the more suspicious side first).
4. Inject contrast at 2–3 mL/s with saline flush.
5. Acquire sequence: MLO affected → MLO contralateral → CC affected → CC contralateral, all within ~7 minutes.
6. Maintain consistent compression; avoid motion between LE/HE pairs.
7. Review for motion; repeat within enhancement window if needed.

## Interpretation Approach

**Read LE images first** using standard mammographic BI-RADS descriptors (mass shape/margin/density; calcification morphology and distribution; architectural distortion).

**Then read recombined images:**
- Background parenchymal enhancement (BPE): minimal, mild, moderate, marked.
- Lesion enhancement: focus (<5 mm), mass, or non-mass enhancement (NME).
- Mass internal enhancement: homogeneous, heterogeneous, **rim (suspicious)**, septations.
- NME distribution: focal, linear, **segmental (suspicious for DCIS)**, regional, diffuse.
- Relative intensity: none, mild, moderate, marked.

**BI-RADS final categories integrate LE, enhancement and ultrasound:**

| Category | Meaning | Malignancy risk |
|----------|---------|-----------------|
| 1 | Negative | 0% |
| 2 | Benign | 0% |
| 3 | Probably benign | <<<2%>> |
| 4A/4B/4C | Low / moderate / high suspicion | 2–95% |
| 5 | Highly suggestive of malignancy | <<≥95%>> |
| 6 | Known biopsy-proven malignancy | — |

## Common Pitfalls

- **Misregistration/motion artefact:** rim or "tramline" enhancement at edges.
- **High BPE:** premenopausal, luteal phase, HRT — schedule <<days 7–14 of the cycle and consider withholding HRT 4–6 weeks before CEM if feasible>>.
- **Vascular enhancement** mimicking linear NME: trace continuity with vessels on LE.
- **Post-biopsy/radiotherapy** inflammation: document interval from intervention.
- **Low-grade DCIS may not enhance** — do not downstage based on absent enhancement; LE morphology governs.
- **Skin/nipple enhancement** is normal.
- **Clip artefact:** bright focus on recombined images.

## Where CEM Fits vs MRI

- CEM has sensitivity approaching MRI for invasive cancer and high-grade DCIS, with shorter exam time and lower cost.
- MRI remains preferred for: implant integrity, chest wall involvement, detailed kinetic assessment, and screening in the highest-risk groups (e.g., BRCA carriers).

{% tab chapter-5 Core Needle Biopsy %}

## General Principles and Safety

**Indications:**
- Sampling BI-RADS 4–5 lesions; selected BI-RADS 3 with risk factors.
- Preoperative diagnosis; axillary nodal staging; concordance for symptomatic lesions.

**Modality choice:**
- **Ultrasound guidance** first-line if lesion visible.
- **Stereotactic/tomosynthesis guidance** for calcifications or distortion not seen on US.
- **MRI-guided biopsy** for MRI-only lesions.

**Consent:** discuss benefits, alternatives, and risks (pain, bruising/haematoma, <<infection rate <1%>>, bleeding, vasovagal syncope, very rare pneumothorax, clip migration, need for further procedures).

## Anticoagulation and Haemostasis

Local policy varies; typical thresholds:

| Agent | Management |
|-------|------------|
| Platelets | <<Proceed if >50 × 10⁹/L; prefer >75 × 10⁹/L for VAB/VAE>> |
| Warfarin | <<Hold to INR <1.5 for core/VAB>> |
| DOACs | <<Omit 24 h before if CrCl ≥50 mL/min; 48 h if renal impairment>> |
| Aspirin | Usually continue |
| Clopidogrel | Consider holding 5–7 days for VAB/VAE if safe |

## Ultrasound-Guided CNB: Step-by-Step

**Equipment:** 14G automated spring-loaded core device; coaxial introducer optional; linear high-frequency probe; sterile sheath; clip device.

1. Position patient comfortably with arm abducted.
2. Identify lesion in two planes; optimise depth, gain; use Doppler to avoid vessels.
3. Plan an in-plane approach along the long axis of the lesion; mark skin.
4. Skin prep and drape; infiltrate LA (1% lidocaine, typical total 5–10 mL) to skin and along the anticipated tract to the deep margin of the lesion.
5. Small skin nick with 11 blade if needed.
6. Advance needle under continuous visualisation — keep the echogenic tip in-plane at all times.
7. Fire device when the sampling notch traverses the target; **take 3–5 cores for most solid masses**.
8. For US-visible calcifications, obtain a specimen radiograph.
9. Deploy a marker clip where appropriate (especially before NAST, for calcifications, or when complete removal possible).
10. Haemostasis: firm compression for 10–15 minutes; steri-strips and dressing.
11. Counsel on aftercare and red flags.

## Tips and Pitfalls

- Always see the needle tip — out-of-plane passes are the commonest cause of misses.
- Use "heel-toe" and hydrodissection for safe windows (superficial or chest wall lesions).
- For very superficial targets: shallow angle, skin tenting, or a skin protection device.
- For deep/chest wall lesions: keep the needle parallel to chest wall to minimise pneumothorax risk.

## Fine-Needle Aspiration (FNA)

- 21–23G needle; useful for cyst aspiration and selected axillary node cytology.
- Limitations: lower sensitivity/specificity vs core; no architectural assessment; not preferred for solid masses.

## Axillary Interventions

**Suspicious node features on ultrasound:**
- <<Cortical thickness >3 mm (focal or diffuse).>>
- Focal cortical bulge/asymmetry.
- Loss/replacement of fatty hilum.
- Round shape and non-hilar cortical vascularity.

**Technique:**
- Core biopsy (14G) preferred for histology.
- **Clip the biopsied positive node** to facilitate targeted axillary dissection (TAD) after NAST.

## UK B-Categorisation

| Category | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| B1 | Normal tissue/inadequate |
| B2 | Benign |
| B3 | Lesion of uncertain malignant potential |
| B4 | Suspicious |
| B5a / B5b | Malignant in situ / invasive |

**Concordance is essential:** discordant benign histology requires repeat biopsy (consider VAB), alternative modality, or excision.

## Complications

- **Haematoma:** compression; cold packs 24–48 h; analgesia.
- **Infection:** <<<1% incidence>>; treat cellulitis with oral antibiotics.
- **Vasovagal syncope:** trendelenburg, oxygen, monitor.
- **Pneumothorax:** very rare; suspect with chest pain/dyspnoea; obtain CXR if indicated.

{% tab chapter-5 Vacuum-Assisted Biopsy %}

## Indications

- Sampling architectural distortion and calcifications (especially stereotactic/tomosynthesis-guided).
- Larger volume sampling where CNB is insufficient.
- B3 lesions for upgrade assessment (VAB) or therapeutic vacuum-assisted excision (VAE).
- Symptomatic benign lesions (e.g., fibroadenoma) for excision.

**Needles:** typically 7–10G (VAE) or 9–12G (VAB).

## Technique (US- or Stereo-Guided)

1. Plan trajectory; infiltrate LA; small skin incision.
2. Advance probe; confirm aperture orientation facing the lesion.
3. Acquire sequential samples circumferentially by rotating the device.
4. For calcifications: perform **specimen radiography**; continue sampling until calcifications are cleared or significantly reduced; document.
5. **Clip placement is mandatory** after VAB/VAE.
6. Compression 15–20 minutes; pressure dressing; consider elastic binder for large cavities.

## Stereotactic/Tomosynthesis-Guided Biopsy

Options: upright add-on, prone table, or tomosynthesis-guided systems.

1. Position and compress with lesion centred; minimise motion.
2. Acquire stereotactic pair (e.g., **±15°**) or tomosynthesis sweep; calculate X, Y, Z coordinates.
3. LA and skin nick; advance needle to calculated Z-depth; pre-fire check image.
4. Fire device (core ≥6 samples) or advance VAB probe; for calcifications verify with specimen radiography.
5. Place clip; **confirm position with two-view mammography**.

**Pitfalls:**
- Thin compressed breast → risk of skin breach; reduce compression once needle is in place.
- Target shift with motion — coach patient.
- For distortion, target the centre on the tomosynthesis slice where most prominent.

## MRI-Guided Biopsy (Outline)

- **Indication:** MRI-only enhancing lesion without ultrasound or mammographic correlate.
- Requires dedicated breast coil, targeting grid, non-ferromagnetic needles, post-contrast imaging.
- Steps: pre-contrast localisation → contrast → identify and plan → VAB sample → MRI-safe clip → confirmation series.

## CEM-Guided Biopsy/Localisation (Emerging)

- Some platforms enable CEM-guided biopsy or localisation for CEM-only enhancing lesions without US/mammographic correlates.
- Refer to centres with capability if finding is actionable.

## Preoperative Localisation Techniques

**Wire-guided localisation (WGL):**
- US or stereo guided; aim for <<wire tip 5–10 mm beyond the lesion>>; avoid trans-nipple paths.
- Same-day placement preferred.

**Seed and tag systems:**
- **Iodine-125 seed (RSL):** strict chain-of-custody and radiation governance; intraoperative gamma probe; confirm retrieval.
- **Magnetic seed (Magseed), radar reflector (SAVI SCOUT), RF tag (LOCalizer):** non-radioactive; <<avoid placement very close to skin (<10 mm) to minimise detector interference and palpability>>.
- Advantages: scheduling flexibility; multiple lesions can be marked.

**ROLL (radioguided occult lesion localisation):**
- Tc-99m-labelled tracer; intraoperative gamma probe guidance.

**Carbon track:**
- Sterile charcoal tattoo of the tract; risk of skin staining.

## Clip Placement and Verification

- Place after any biopsy likely to alter lesion appearance (tumours before NAST, calcifications, VAB/VAE).
- **Avoid deploying into a haematoma** (migration risk).
- Post-deployment: **two-view mammogram** to confirm position; document distance from nipple and quadrant.

## Specimen Handling

- For calcifications: specimen radiography is mandatory; indicate calcification density; send radiograph with pathology request.
- Communicate radiology suspicion to pathology — **radiology–pathology correlation is critical**.

## Post-Procedure Aftercare

- Keep dressing dry for 24–48 hours.
- Expect bruising/tenderness for 1–2 weeks.
- **Red flags:** rapidly enlarging swelling, severe uncontrolled pain, fever, purulent discharge, dyspnoea — urgent review.
- Avoid heavy lifting with affected arm for 24–48 hours; supportive bra.
- Restart anticoagulants typically 24 hours post-procedure once haemostasis is secure.
