Breast implants and the treated breast present unique interpretive challenges, requiring clarity on surgical history, implant characteristics and expected temporal evolution of findings. This chapter outlines a structured approach to optimising technique, recognising expected post-operative and post-radiotherapy appearances, diagnosing implant complications and escalating appropriately to the multidisciplinary team.

{% tab chapter-11 Implant Assessment %}

## Implant Types and Position

**Implant types**
- **Silicone gel-filled** (most common): cohesive ("gummy bear") or less cohesive gel.
- **Saline-filled**: deflate visibly with rupture; minimal imaging role if clinically obvious.
- **Tissue expanders**: temporary, often with magnetic ports — <<may be MR unsafe or MR conditional; always check manufacturer documentation before MRI>>.
- **Textured vs smooth shell**: <<textured shells are associated with increased risk of BIA-ALCL>>.

**Implant position**
- **Subglandular (retromammary)**: more parenchymal masking; harder Eklund views if contractured.
- **Subpectoral (retropectoral/dual plane)**: easier displacement for Eklund views; less parenchymal masking.

**Reconstruction context**
- Implant-based (prepectoral or retropectoral, ± acellular dermal matrix).
- Autologous flap (DIEP, TRAM, LD): variable fat/fibrous composition; routine mammography usually not indicated unless symptomatic.

## Mammography in the Augmented Breast

**Views**
- Standard CC and MLO for each breast.
- **Implant-displaced (Eklund) CC and MLO** views where feasible — <<a bilaterally augmented patient typically requires 8 views in total>>.

**Eklund technique — step by step**
1. Position as for standard view.
2. Manually displace the implant posteriorly and superiorly toward the chest wall.
3. Pull native breast tissue anteriorly onto the detector.
4. Apply firm but tolerable compression to the displaced parenchyma only.
5. Acquire image; ensure nipple in profile with adequate posterior tissue.

**Optimisation**
- Subpectoral implants displace more readily than subglandular.
- <<Baker grade III–IV capsular contracture may preclude successful Eklund views — document the limitation>>.
- Use **digital breast tomosynthesis (DBT)** where available to reduce summation artefact.

## Ultrasound Assessment

- High-frequency linear probe (12–18 MHz); light transducer pressure; generous gel.
- Survey all quadrants, retroareolar region, implant surface and axillae.
- Evaluate: shell contour and internal lines, peri-implant space, capsule thickness, parenchyma, axillary nodes (look for "snowstorm" silicone artefact).

## MRI Protocol Selection

- **Integrity MRI (non-contrast)**: T2-weighted plus silicone-specific and silicone-suppressed sequences (often Dixon-based); 3D sequences for shell evaluation.
- **Cancer/problem-solving MRI**: dynamic contrast-enhanced (DCE) protocol ± DWI.
- <<Routine MRI surveillance of asymptomatic silicone implants is not standard NHS practice — follow local policy>>.

## BI-RADS Reporting

- Use BI-RADS for malignancy likelihood; describe implant findings separately.
- **Isolated implant rupture without suspicious parenchymal findings is BI-RADS 2**.
- Always document: implant type and position (if known), capsular findings, integrity signs, peri-implant fluid, axillary nodes and technical limitations.

{% tab chapter-11 Implant Failure %}

## Capsular Contracture

**Baker grades (clinical)**
| Grade | Features |
|-------|----------|
| I | Soft, natural |
| II | Slightly firm, normal appearance |
| III | Firm, distorted |
| IV | Painful, distorted |

**Imaging**
- Mammography: rounded/oval implant, increased density; sometimes calcified capsule.
- US: thickened echogenic capsule with radial folds.
- MRI: compressed/rounded implant without rupture signs.

## Silicone Rupture

**Intracapsular rupture**
- **US**: *Stepladder sign* — multiple parallel echogenic lines within implant; keyhole/noose/teardrop sign.
- **MRI**: *Linguine sign* — collapsed wavy shell fragments floating within silicone (most specific); *subcapsular line sign*; keyhole sign.
- Mammography is limited for intracapsular rupture.

**Extracapsular rupture**
- **US**: *Snowstorm artefact* — echogenic foci with dirty posterior shadowing in soft tissues or nodes.
- Mammography: dense irregular/loculated opacities; silicone-laden nodes (dense, loss of fatty hilum).
- MRI: free silicone signal outside the capsule on silicone-specific sequences.

**Saline rupture**
- Clinically obvious deflation; collapsed shell on imaging; fluid resorbed.

## Other Implant Complications

- **Malposition, rotation, double capsule, gel bleed**: MRI best characterises; correlate with symptoms.
- **Infection/haematoma**: usually early (<6 weeks); US ± aspiration with microbiology.
- **Silicone lymphadenopathy**: snowstorm artefact in nodes; <<typical silicone lymphadenopathy without a suspicious breast lesion is BI-RADS 2>>.

## Breast Implant–Associated ALCL (BIA-ALCL)

**Key facts**
- Rare CD30-positive, ALK-negative T-cell lymphoma.
- <<Associated predominantly with textured implants; median time to presentation ≈ 8–10 years post-implantation>>.
- Most common presentation: **late peri-implant seroma (>1 year post-insertion)**; less commonly a capsular mass or axillary node.

**Imaging pathway**
1. **Ultrasound first-line**: identify peri-implant fluid and capsular masses; assess axillary nodes.
2. **Ultrasound-guided aspiration** of fluid: <<aim to aspirate ≥20–50 mL where feasible, sending for cytology, CD30 immunohistochemistry (positive), ALK (negative), flow cytometry and microbiology per local protocol>>.
3. **US-guided core biopsy** of any capsular mass; mark the site.
4. If confirmed: MRI for local extent; staging with PET-CT/CT.

**Management**
- MDT with plastics and haemato-oncology.
- En bloc capsulectomy and implant removal for disease confined to capsule/effusion.
- Systemic therapy for invasive or advanced disease.

## Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Performing MRI on an MR-unsafe expander** — always confirm device status.
- **Dismissing a late seroma as benign** — <<any new peri-implant effusion more than 1 year after implant insertion warrants aspiration with CD30 immunocytochemistry to exclude BIA-ALCL>>.
- **Confusing silicone-laden nodes with metastatic nodes** — look for snowstorm artefact; FNA/core if uncertain.

## Expected Temporal Changes

| Time from treatment | Expected findings |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| 0–6 weeks post-op | Seroma/haematoma, oedema, skin thickening, architectural distortion at lumpectomy bed |
| 3–6 months post-RT | Peak skin thickening, trabecular oedema, increased density |
| 1–3 years post-RT | Gradual regression of oedema/enhancement |
| **>12–18 months** | <<Most inflammatory enhancement and oedema should have regressed; new or persistent focal abnormalities are concerning>> |

## Mammographic Surveillance after BCS

- **Baseline post-treatment mammogram: 6–12 months after completion of radiotherapy** (or surgery if no RT).
- Annual surveillance thereafter; local policy may vary for higher-risk patients.
- Expected findings:
  - Surgical clips marking cavity.
  - Architectural distortion/scar that **stabilises or decreases** over time.
  - Benign calcifications: fat necrosis (coarse, rim/eggshell, lucent-centred), dystrophic.
- **Suspicious**: new/amorphous, pleomorphic, linear/segmental calcifications — biopsy.

## Distinguishing Scar, Fat Necrosis and Recurrence

| Feature | Scar | Fat necrosis | Recurrence |
|---------|------|--------------|------------|
| Mammography | Distortion tethered to skin; **decreases over time** | Oil cyst with rim/eggshell calcification; lucent-centred coarse calcifications | New mass, increasing distortion, suspicious calcifications |
| US | Echogenic distortion, no discrete mass, avascular | Complex cystic mass, echogenic bands, fat–fluid levels | New hypoechoic mass with internal vascularity |
| MRI | Minimal/no early enhancement; decreases serially | Central fat signal; thin rim enhancement if inflamed | Mass with irregular margins, early wash-in, **Type III washout kinetics**, restricted diffusion |

## MRI in the Treated Breast

- **Timing**: <<prefer MRI ≥6–12 months after surgery/radiotherapy to reduce false positives, unless clinical urgency dictates earlier imaging>>.
- **Kinetic curves (BI-RADS)**:
  - Type I (persistent): more often benign.
  - Type II (plateau): indeterminate.
  - **Type III (washout): suspicious**.
- **DWI**: <<malignant lesions often show ADC <1.3 × 10⁻³ mm²/s, though overlap exists — always integrate with morphology and kinetics>>.
- Early rim enhancement of a lumpectomy seroma cavity can be normal up to 12–18 months.

## Axillary Assessment Post-treatment

- Post-SNB/clearance: fluid collections, lymphocoeles, neuromas; altered nodal morphology.
- **Suspicious node**: <<cortical thickness >3 mm, focal cortical bulge, loss of fatty hilum, rounded hypoechoic morphology — biopsy>>.

## After Mastectomy and Reconstruction

- **No routine mammography for the reconstructed side post-mastectomy**; image new symptoms with US first, MRI if equivocal.
- Recurrence patterns: skin/subcutaneous or chest wall nodules along scar line; less commonly axilla or internal mammary chain.
- Nipple-sparing mastectomy: residual retroareolar tissue may be present.

## Post-neoadjuvant Therapy (NACT)

- **Pre-NACT**: clip the primary tumour and any biopsied positive node.
- **Response assessment**: MRI is the single best modality (size, enhancement, non-mass patterns).
- **Calcifications may persist despite a complete pathological response** — do not equate persistent calcifications with viable disease without correlation.
- Surgical planning: localise clipped node for targeted axillary dissection; bracket residual disease using clip position.

## MDT Triggers

- Suspicious imaging for recurrence or new primary.
- Indeterminate post-treatment change where further imaging or biopsy alters management.
- Confirmed/suspected BIA-ALCL.
- Complex implant complications requiring surgical planning.
- Response assessment influencing surgical strategy post-NACT.
- Equivocal post-treatment axillary findings.

## Key Take-Aways

- **Always attempt Eklund (implant-displaced) views** in augmented patients and use DBT where available; document failure when capsular contracture precludes adequate displacement.
- The **linguine sign on MRI** is the most specific finding for intracapsular silicone rupture; **snowstorm artefact on ultrasound** signifies extracapsular silicone.
- A **new peri-implant effusion more than 1 year after implant insertion** must be aspirated (≥20–50 mL) and sent for cytology and CD30 immunocytochemistry to exclude BIA-ALCL.
- Baseline post-BCS mammography is performed **6–12 months after completion of radiotherapy**; problem-solving MRI is best deferred to ≥6–12 months post-treatment unless clinical urgency dictates otherwise.
- Distinguish scar (stable/regressing, minimal enhancement) from recurrence (new or enlarging mass, Type III washout kinetics, restricted diffusion) and fat necrosis (rim/eggshell calcification, lucent-centred mass).
- Persistent calcifications after NACT do not equate to residual viable tumour — correlate with MRI and biopsy suspicious morphology.
