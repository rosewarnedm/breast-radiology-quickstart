## Why a system matters

Breast imaging is simultaneously high-volume and high-stakes. Lesions that matter are sometimes subtle; lesions that are obvious are sometimes benign. Without a repeatable method, you rely on pattern recognition alone — which works well for classic cases but fails exactly where it is most needed: the edge case, the subtle developing change, the second lesion found after an obvious first one. A disciplined system reduces variability, counteracts cognitive bias, and supports defensible decisions whether you are sitting at a NHSBSP screening workstation or seeing a symptomatic patient in an assessment clinic.

The goal of this chapter is not to hand you a list of signs to memorise. It is to show you how to think about breast imaging: why appearances arise, how modalities complement one another, and how to reason from image to management within UK pathways.

## The UK framework you are working within

Before interpreting a single image, ground yourself in the context that shapes every decision.

**The NHSBSP** invites women aged 50–70 every three years for two-view mammography (craniocaudal [CC] and mediolateral oblique [MLO]). Women over 70 may self-refer. Double reading with arbitration is standard: two independent readers score independently, and discordance is resolved at arbitration. The programme is calibrated to balance sensitivity against recall rate — both matter, and you will hear this discussed in audit.

**Triple assessment** is the organising principle for both screening recall and symptomatic clinics. The three limbs — clinical examination, imaging, and pathology — each contribute an independent score:

- **P-score** (clinical/palpation): P1 normal → P5 malignant
- **M-score** (mammography) and **U-score** (ultrasound): same 1–5 scale
- **B-score** (core biopsy histology): B1 non-diagnostic, B2 benign, B3 lesion of uncertain malignant potential, B4 suspicious, B5 malignant

The power of this system is concordance: all three limbs pointing in the same direction provides confidence. Discordance is a signal to stop and think.

**Modality roles in UK practice:**

| Modality | Primary role |
|---|---|
| Mammography (2D / digital) | Screening backbone; calcifications; architectural distortion |
| Digital breast tomosynthesis (DBT) | Assessment; improves mass margin and distortion conspicuity; reduces summation artefact |
| Ultrasound | First-line for focal symptoms; characterises and targets masses and cysts |
| MRI | Problem solving; extent of disease; implant assessment; high-risk screening |
| Image-guided biopsy | Ultrasound guidance preferred where possible; stereotactic/tomosynthesis for calcifications and distortion; MRI guidance for MRI-only lesions |

DBT is increasingly used in screening at some centres but practice varies — check what your unit does before interpreting studies.

## A four-pass approach

Organise your reading into four deliberate passes:

1. **Preparation and technical adequacy**
2. **Global overview and context**
3. **Structured search and lesion analysis**
4. **Correlation, concordance, and management**

The temptation — especially early in training — is to jump straight to an obvious abnormality. Resist it. Many misses occur after the first finding is made, a phenomenon called *satisfaction of search*. The system exists precisely to protect against this.

## Pass 1: Preparation and technical adequacy

### Clinical context and comparison

Know why the images were taken before you look at them. In screening, you have limited clinical information by design. In symptomatic practice, you need:

- The precise symptom (lump, pain, nipple discharge, skin change), its site, laterality, and duration
- Risk factors: family history, personal history of breast cancer or high-risk lesion, prior surgery or radiotherapy, implants, HRT use, pregnancy or lactation
- The P-score from clinical examination

**Always compare with prior studies.** This is arguably the single most useful habit in breast imaging. Stability of a finding over years moves it firmly towards benignity; new or developing change demands explanation. Prior studies often convert genuine diagnostic uncertainty into a confident management decision.

### Mammographic positioning and quality

A poorly positioned image cannot be reliably interpreted. Before you report, confirm:

**MLO:** Pectoralis muscle visible to nipple level or below, with a convex anterior border. Inframammary fold open. Adequate posterior tissue included. Minimal skin folds. Nipple in profile where possible.

**CC:** Nipple in profile. Posterior nipple line (PNL) within 1 cm of the MLO PNL — if this difference is greater, posterior tissue has been excluded from one view. Medial tissue included. No motion blur.

Adequate compression matters: it improves contrast, reduces dose, separates overlapping tissue, and helps reveal distortion. If compression is clearly inadequate, note it. If positioning is so poor that a significant lesion could be hidden, request a repeat rather than reporting a suboptimal study.

### DBT and synthetic 2D

DBT reduces tissue overlap by generating a pseudo-three-dimensional dataset from a limited angular arc. When reading tomosynthesis:

- Track lesions through consecutive slices to distinguish true findings from noise or vessel cross-sections
- Be alert to common artefacts: step artefacts at the skin surface, motion streaks, and halo artefacts around dense structures
- Use the synthetic 2D image as a guide but confirm suspicious findings on the DBT slices themselves — and equally, do not recall artefacts on synthetic 2D that disappear on tomosynthesis slices

### Ultrasound technical adequacy

Use a high-frequency linear transducer (typically 12–18 MHz). Before scanning a lesion, ensure:

- Depth set so the lesion is in the upper half of the field of view with posterior features visible
- Focus at or just below the lesion
- Gentle pressure — excess pressure compresses posteriorly shadowing lesions and degrades posterior enhancement
- Gain and time-gain compensation adjusted so the fat layer has a uniform, low-level echo background

Document each finding with clockface position, distance from nipple, size in three planes, and relationship to skin and chest wall. Consistent documentation allows meaningful comparison on follow-up.

### MRI technical adequacy

Before interpreting a breast MRI, confirm:

- High-resolution T1-weighted pre-contrast sequence acquired
- Dynamic contrast-enhanced (DCE) series with adequate temporal resolution for kinetic analysis
- Subtraction images and maximum intensity projection (MIP) available
- T2 or STIR included
- Silicone-sensitive sequences present if implants are in situ
- Images acquired on days 7–14 of the menstrual cycle where possible — background parenchymal enhancement (BPE) is lowest here and false positives are minimised

## Pass 2: Global overview and context

Before examining any individual abnormality, survey the whole study. This establishes the background against which subtle changes become visible.

On mammography, look for:

- **Symmetry** of density distribution between breasts
- **Breast density** — note this because it directly affects sensitivity and may prompt adjunct ultrasound in symptomatic dense breasts
- **Skin and nipple** — oedema, thickening, retraction, or asymmetric flattening
- **Obvious masses, distortions, or calcification clusters**
- **Axilla** — node morphology on the MLO view

On ultrasound, mentally divide the breast into quadrants plus the subareolar region before you begin scanning. On MRI, review the MIP first to locate enhancing foci, then examine source images systematically.

This global pass matters because it tells you what to expect and where to look harder. Dense tissue on mammography, for instance, should prompt extra scrutiny on ultrasound and awareness that a normal mammogram does not exclude a significant lesion. Prior surgery should prompt you to recognise postoperative distortion and fat necrosis rather than calling them malignant.

## Pass 3: Structured search and lesion analysis

Use a consistent anatomical route. On mammography and DBT, one practical sequence is:

1. Skin and nipple–areolar complex
2. Subcutaneous fat
3. Fibroglandular tissue — central, then sweeping to the periphery
4. Retroareolar region and ducts
5. Retromammary space and pectoral edge
6. Axillary tail and visualised axilla

Work through both breasts. For ultrasound, scan radially around the nipple, then sweep orthogonally, and always target the symptomatic area and any mammographically depicted site. For MRI, review T2 first (cysts, oedema, seromas), then DCE subtractions and MIPs, then assess morphology and kinetics for each enhancing lesion.

Within this search, classify every finding into one or more of the four mammographic lesion types — **mass, calcifications, architectural distortion, or asymmetry** — and analyse using standard descriptors. The same descriptive logic carries across DBT, ultrasound, and MRI.

### Masses

**Mammographic descriptors:**

- *Shape:* round/oval/lobulated (more likely benign) versus irregular (suspicious)
- *Margins:* circumscribed versus obscured, indistinct, microlobulated, angular, or spiculated
- *Density:* fat-density lesions are nearly always benign; high soft-tissue density, particularly if higher than the surrounding parenchyma, can be suspicious
- *Associated features:* spicules, skin or nipple retraction, trabecular thickening, calcifications, axillary node change

**The principle underlying margin assessment:** spiculated margins reflect a desmoplastic stromal response to infiltrative tumour growth. Understanding this means you are not just matching a pattern to a diagnosis — you understand why spiculation matters, which helps you handle the case where spiculation is subtle or where a benign lesion (such as a radial scar) produces a similar appearance.

**Ultrasound analysis of a mass:**

- *Echo pattern:* anechoic (simple cyst), complex cystic and solid, hypoechoic solid, heterogeneous
- *Margins and shape:* circumscribed versus angular or microlobulated; wider-than-tall (parallel orientation) is generally reassuring; taller-than-wide (non-parallel) is suspicious because lesions growing against tissue planes rather than along them tend to be invasive
- *Posterior features:* posterior enhancement favours a cyst or some malignancies; posterior shadowing suggests desmoplastic reaction but also occurs with benign sclerosing lesions — neither feature should be used in isolation
- *Internal vascularity:* Doppler flow within a structure confirms it is solid; absence of flow does not exclude malignancy

**Key differentials and discriminating features:**

*Cyst versus solid:* A simple cyst is anechoic, has imperceptible walls, and shows posterior enhancement and edge shadowing. Debris that layers or shifts with repositioning suggests a complicated cyst. Internal vascularity on Doppler confirms a solid component.

*Fibroadenoma versus phyllodes:* Both are lobulated, circumscribed, hypoechoic solid masses. Phyllodes is suggested by rapid growth, size above 3–4 cm, lobulated contour with internal cystic spaces, and heterogeneous echogenicity. The distinction is ultimately histological — if you suspect phyllodes, note it in your report.

*Intramammary lymph node:* Look for a reniform shape with a fatty hilum on mammography and a thin peripheral hypoechoic cortex with preserved fatty hilum on ultrasound. Location in the upper outer quadrant or axillary tail is typical. Cortical thickening or asymmetry compared with the contralateral side should prompt further assessment.

*Fat necrosis:* Can mimic almost anything from a benign oil cyst (fat density on mammography, thin rim calcification) to a spiculated mass on ultrasound or MRI. The key is correlation with history — prior surgery, trauma, or radiotherapy — and recognition of the oil cyst or rimming calcification pattern. Without a clear history, biopsy is often appropriate.

**Pitfalls:**

A common one: some high-grade malignancies — particularly triple-negative breast cancers — grow in an expansile rather than infiltrative fashion and appear as round, circumscribed masses mimicking fibroadenomas. The combination of interval growth, younger age, clinical firmness, and internal heterogeneity on ultrasound should raise suspicion even when margins appear smooth.

Summation masses on 2D mammography are common and are the classic false positive. DBT almost always clarifies whether a mass is real. Use spot compression if DBT is not available.

Nipple and skin lesions can project over the breast parenchyma. When in doubt, apply a radio-opaque marker, request a tangential view, and use ultrasound to confirm epidermal or dermal origin.

### Calcifications

Calcifications are one area where mammography is genuinely superior to other modalities and where morphological analysis is central.

**Morphological categories:**

*Typically benign:* Skin calcifications (lucent centre, often grouped at skin); vascular calcifications (parallel tram-track pattern following vessels, usually bilateral); coarse "popcorn" calcifications (involuting fibroadenomas); large rod-like or secretory calcifications (duct ectasia, radiating toward the nipple); rim or eggshell calcifications (fat necrosis or cyst walls); milk of calcium (teacup layering on true lateral view, amorphous on CC).

*Suspicious:* Fine pleomorphic calcifications (variable size and shape, no benign explanation); fine linear or branching "casting" calcifications (filling ductal lumens, following a segmental distribution).

**The underlying principle:** Fine pleomorphic and fine linear calcifications are deposited in necrotic tumour debris within ducts. Their morphology and distribution therefore mirror the ductal anatomy. When you see segmental distribution with fine linear forms, you are effectively imaging high-grade DCIS tracking along a ductal system. Understanding this makes it easier to appreciate why distribution matters as much as morphology.

**Distribution matters:**

- *Grouped:* ≥5 calcifications within 1 cm — the size boundary that traditionally triggers further assessment
- *Linear or ductal:* following a duct toward the nipple
- *Segmental:* conforming to a ductal segment — the most suspicious distribution because it implies extensive intraductal disease

**Correlation and management:**

Always use magnification views to refine morphology before assigning a score or planning biopsy. Ultrasound is rarely helpful for DCIS-associated calcifications unless there is a coexistent mass; however, it may demonstrate ductal change in cases of duct ectasia. For suspicious calcifications, stereotactic or tomosynthesis-guided vacuum-assisted biopsy is standard, with specimen radiography to confirm retrieval.

**Pitfalls:**

Skin calcifications are the most common false positive — apply a marker, take a tangential view. Vascular calcifications can superficially resemble fine linear DCIS but follow vessels, are typically bilateral, and are often paired in a parallel pattern. Milk of calcium is a benign entity that is genuinely morphologically amorphous on CC views — always take a true lateral if you suspect this diagnosis.

### Architectural distortion

Architectural distortion is defined as focal disruption of normal parenchymal architecture — radiating lines and focal retraction — without a definable central mass. It is one of the most commonly missed mammographic signs, and DBT has substantially improved both its detection and the confidence with which it can be confirmed.

**What causes it?** Malignant distortion arises from tumour infiltration and the associated stromal desmoplastic response pulling Cooper's ligaments and parenchymal tissue inward. Benign causes include radial scar/complex sclerosing lesion, postoperative scarring, sclerosing adenosis, and focal fibrosis.

**Ultrasound findings:** Ultrasound may demonstrate a subtle hypoechoic shadowing area without a discrete mass, or may be entirely negative. Use light probe pressure — excessive pressure can compress the abnormality out of view.

**Management:** Any distortion without a surgical explanation at that site requires biopsy. Radial scars, even when confirmed histologically, carry an upgrade risk (both to B3 entities and occasionally to malignancy) and most UK units proceed to vacuum excision or surgical biopsy, though practice varies — check your local protocol.

Overlapping Cooper's ligaments and summation lines on 2D mammography can exactly mimic distortion. Confirm on both standard views, then use DBT or spot compression. A true distortion persists; summation artefact resolves.
