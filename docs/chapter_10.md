# Chapter 10: Screening and Symptomatic Interpretation

---

## Overview

Breast imaging in the UK operates across two distinct but interlinked pathways: the NHS Breast Screening Programme (NHSBSP), which seeks to detect cancer before it becomes clinically apparent in asymptomatic women, and the symptomatic service, which investigates patients presenting with breast complaints or known high-risk backgrounds. These contexts demand different prior probabilities, different recall thresholds, and often different modality choices — and conflating them is a common source of interpretive error.

This chapter is not a catalogue of appearances to memorise. Instead, it aims to build an interpretive framework: understanding *why* findings look as they do, how to communicate uncertainty consistently, and how to integrate imaging with clinical assessment and pathology in a safe, systematic way. Everything here is anchored in UK practice — double reading with arbitration, triple assessment, and the five-point P/M/U/B scoring system.

## The UK Pathways at a Glance

### NHS Breast Screening Programme

The NHSBSP invites women aged 50 to their 71st birthday for two-view digital mammography (craniocaudal and mediolateral oblique) every three years. Many areas have extended this to 47–73 years as part of the Age Extension trial. Eligibility for trans and non-binary individuals depends on birth sex, gender-affirming treatment and GP registration status — follow your local policy.

Every screen is independently read by two trained readers. Where they agree, the result stands; where they disagree, a third reader or senior radiologist arbitrates. This double-reading model increases cancer detection compared with single reading and provides a quality check, but it also demands that each reader form an independent opinion before any comparison.

Programme quality is measured through recall rate, cancer detection rate (CDR), positive predictive value (PPV) of recall, and interval cancer rates. These metrics exist in tension: a lower recall threshold catches more cancers but generates more false positives and patient anxiety. Programme standards aim to optimise this balance, and being aware of your own recall metrics is part of professional development.

Women recalled from screening attend a dedicated assessment clinic offering additional mammographic views, tomosynthesis, targeted ultrasound and image-guided biopsy — all within a triple assessment framework.

### Symptomatic Service and Triple Assessment

Symptomatic patients are seen through urgent (two-week-wait) and routine one-stop clinics. Triple assessment combines:

- **Clinical examination**, scored P1–P5
- **Imaging** — mammography (M1–M5) and/or ultrasound (U1–U5), with MRI selectively
- **Tissue sampling**, reported as B1–B5

The scoring system standardises communication:

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 | Normal |
| 2 | Benign |
| 3 | Indeterminate / probably benign |
| 4 | Suspicious of malignancy |
| 5 | Highly suspicious / malignant |

For histology: B2 = benign; B3 = uncertain malignant potential; B4 = suspicious; B5a = DCIS; B5b = invasive carcinoma.

The power of triple assessment lies in integration. A P4 lump with benign imaging (M2/U2) is discordant — that discordance should prompt further action, not reassurance. Conversely, concordant benign findings across all three arms support safe discharge. Develop the habit of explicitly asking: *do these three arms agree, and if not, why not?*

## Anatomy and Physics: Why Things Look the Way They Do

Before pattern recognition, understand the principles. This is what allows you to generalise to cases you have never seen before.

### Breast Anatomy and Its Imaging Correlates

The breast is a radially organised glandular structure embedded in fat, supported by Cooper's ligaments that span from skin to pectoral fascia. Lobules and ducts converge towards the nipple–areolar complex. This architecture has direct imaging consequences:

- **Desmoplasia** — the fibrotic stromal reaction to malignancy — tethers Cooper's ligaments, producing the spiculated margin that is the most reliable mammographic sign of malignancy
- **Ductal anatomy** explains why DCIS often produces linear or segmental calcification distributions — calcification maps the necrotic intraluminal contents within a lobar unit
- **Axillary nodal drainage** (levels I–III) explains why nodal assessment is integral to staging

Breast density (the proportion of fibroglandular to fatty tissue) varies with age, hormonal status, parity and medications. Dense tissue attenuates X-rays similarly to soft tissue masses, reducing mammographic sensitivity. This is not a failing of interpretation — it is a physical limitation of the technique, and acknowledging it in your report is appropriate practice.

### Modality Physics in Brief

**Mammography** is a projection technique: everything along the X-ray beam is summed into a single image. Its strengths are microcalcification detection and demonstration of subtle architectural change. Its principal weakness is superimposition — overlapping tissue can mask a real lesion, or create a pseudolesion where none exists.

**Digital breast tomosynthesis (DBT)** acquires images across a limited arc and reconstructs thin slices, effectively "subtracting" overlapping tissue. This substantially improves mass and distortion conspicuity and reduces recalls for summation artefact. However — and this is important — microcalcification analysis on tomosynthesis is less reliable than on magnified two-dimensional mammography. Do not use tomosynthesis slices to characterise calcifications; always use magnified 2D views.

**Ultrasound** uses acoustic impedance differences between tissues. Benign lesions tend to be oval, parallel to the skin surface (wider-than-tall), with circumscribed margins and homogeneous echotexture. Malignant masses are more often irregular, non-parallel (taller-than-wide), with angular or spiculated margins and posterior acoustic shadowing — the ultrasound equivalent of desmoplasia disrupting acoustic transmission. These are probabilities, not rules; always integrate with clinical and mammographic findings.

**MRI** detects tumour angiogenesis through dynamic gadolinium enhancement, supplemented by diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI). High b-value restricted diffusion increases specificity for malignancy. MRI has the highest sensitivity of any breast imaging modality but variable specificity; use it in the right clinical context (extent assessment, occult primary, high-risk screening, post-neoadjuvant therapy) rather than as a routine problem-solver.

**Contrast-enhanced mammography (CEM)** uses dual-energy iodinated contrast to visualise enhancement on a mammographic platform. It is emerging as a practical alternative to MRI for problem solving and extent assessment in some centres, though it is not yet routine in screening.

## Principles of Screening Interpretation

### The Right Mindset

When reading screening mammograms, the disease prevalence in your study population is low — roughly 6–8 cancers per 1,000 screens in the UK. Most of what you see is benign. The corollary is that a finding needs genuinely suspicious features to justify recall; recalling women for clearly benign findings wastes resource and causes unnecessary anxiety. However, the consequence of a missed cancer is serious — so your threshold must remain appropriately sensitive to suspicious morphology.

This is a different mindset from symptomatic practice, where prior probability is higher and the threshold for investigation shifts accordingly.

### A Systematic Search Pattern

Consistent technique prevents errors of omission. Develop a search pattern and use it every time:

1. **Image quality and positioning** — assess before interpreting. Is the posterior tissue adequate? Is the pectoral muscle visible to nipple level on the MLO? Is the nipple in profile?
2. **Global density and symmetry** — note the density category; compare sides at the same level
3. **Nipple–areolar complex and subareolar region**
4. **Posterior tissue and retroglandular fat**
5. **Each quadrant systematically, including axillary tail**
6. **Skin, trabecular pattern, axillae**
7. **Comparison with prior examinations** — change over time is one of the most powerful discriminators available to you

### Key Screening Findings

#### Masses

Describe every mass by shape, margin and density relative to fibroglandular tissue.

- **Round/oval, circumscribed, equal or low density**: favours benign (cyst, fibroadenoma, intramammary node). If stable and classic, no recall is needed.
- **Spiculated margin**: the most specific sign of malignancy on mammography, arising from desmoplastic tethering of Cooper's ligaments. Even a small spiculated lesion warrants recall.
- **Indistinct, microlobulated or obscured margins**: intermediate concern; ultrasound and often biopsy required
- **High density relative to fibroglandular tissue**: increases suspicion
- **Associated calcifications within a mass**: increases suspicion substantially

The key principle is that *margin* is more informative than *size*. A 6 mm spiculated lesion is more concerning than a 2 cm circumscribed one.

Ultrasound is the natural complement to mammographic mass assessment: it distinguishes cystic from solid, refines margin characterisation, and provides real-time biopsy guidance. DBT can down-stage an apparently obscured circumscribed mass by revealing circumscribed margins free of overlap — reducing the need for recall in some cases.

#### Calcifications

Calcifications are one of the most important screening findings and one of the most common sources of both unnecessary recall and missed malignancy. The key variables are **morphology** and **distribution** — assess both before drawing a conclusion.

**Benign morphologies** to recognise confidently and not recall:
- Skin calcifications — superficial, often paired; confirm with tangential view
- Vascular calcifications — parallel tram-track lines
- Coarse or "popcorn" calcifications — involuting fibroadenoma
- Rim or eggshell calcifications — fat necrosis
- Lucent-centred calcifications
- Milk of calcium — characteristically settles on lateral views (teacup appearance) and appears smudged on the CC; always look on the lateral

**Suspicious morphologies requiring assessment**:
- Fine pleomorphic — irregular particles of varying size and shape
- Fine linear and branching (casting) — calcification moulding the duct lumen, highly predictive of high-grade DCIS

**Distribution matters** at least as much as morphology:
- Diffuse/bilateral/scattered — almost always benign
- Grouped/clustered — scrutinise morphology carefully; suspicious if morphology is indeterminate or worse
- Segmental or linear — tracks the ductal tree of one lobe; higher risk for DCIS even with less alarming morphology

Always use **magnification views** to characterise calcifications. Two projections are needed — the lateral helps identify milk of calcium. Never rely on standard views alone for calcification analysis.

#### Architectural Distortion

Architectural distortion is a focal disruption of the normal parenchymal pattern — lines radiating from a point, or focal retraction — without a definable mass centre. It is a finding that demands respect.

**Causes**: malignancy (including invasive lobular carcinoma, which is notorious for subtle distortion), radial scar/complex sclerosing lesion, postsurgical scar, fat necrosis.

**Key principle**: new distortion in the absence of prior surgery is suspicious until proven otherwise. DBT substantially improves both detection and characterisation of distortion, and has become an important tool in this context. On ultrasound, the correlate is often subtle — an ill-defined hypoechoic area with posterior shadowing; targeted scanning of the relevant area is essential, and a normal ultrasound does not exclude a clinically significant lesion.

Postsurgical scars can mimic distortion, but tend to have a central fatty component, are located where prior surgery occurred, and remain stable or regress over time.

#### Asymmetries

- **Global asymmetry** — larger volume of fibroglandular tissue on one side; usually a normal variant
- **Focal asymmetry** — localised asymmetric tissue without mass characteristics; if persistent and without benign explanation, may warrant assessment
- **Developing asymmetry** — new or increasing focal asymmetry on comparison with prior examinations; this is the most concerning category and should prompt recall

Resolve potential asymmetries with spot compression or DBT before recalling — many represent summation of normal tissue.

#### Associated and Secondary Signs

- Skin thickening, trabecular oedema, trabecular coarsening, nipple change, axillary lymphadenopathy, and vascular engorgement are secondary findings that modify interpretation
- Unilateral oedema pattern should prompt consideration of lymphatic obstruction, either malignant or due to inflammatory causes — clinical correlation is essential
- Inflammatory breast carcinoma presents with rapid onset of skin changes and peau d'orange; this is a clinical and imaging emergency

## Principles of Symptomatic Interpretation

### Adjusting for Context

In symptomatic practice, the pre-test probability of malignancy is higher, the imaging is targeted to the area of concern, and modality choice is guided by clinical presentation and age. You are also working alongside a clinician who has already examined the patient — use that information.

Tailor your approach, but do not develop tunnel vision. Remain alert to incidental findings away from the symptomatic site — the "second lesion" is a real phenomenon.

### Modality Choice by Presentation and Age

**Palpable lump:**
- Under approximately 35: ultrasound first-line. Mammography is added if ultrasound is suspicious or indeterminate, if there are strong risk factors, or if clinical features are concerning.
- 35–39: ultrasound first; mammography on a case-by-case basis.
- 40 and over: two-view mammography plus targeted ultrasound as standard.
- Male patients: ultrasound first; add mammography if suspicious or aged over 50.

The cut-off ages reflect the balance between mammographic sensitivity (reduced in younger, denser breasts) and radiation risk (lower in older women). These are guidelines — apply clinical judgement.

**Nipple discharge:**
- Physiological bilateral milky or coloured discharge without focal signs: no imaging required below age thresholds; reassurance is appropriate.
- Pathological discharge (spontaneous, unilateral, single duct, blood-stained or serous): ultrasound of the subareolar region; mammography if aged 40 or over. If imaging is negative but discharge persists, consider duct excision. MRI can identify occult intraductal lesions in some centres.
- Papilloma is the most common cause and receives a B3 classification; the presence of associated atypia changes management.

**Breast pain:**
- Diffuse cyclical mastalgia without a focal abnormality: no imaging is indicated below standard age thresholds; explanation and reassurance are appropriate.
- Focal persistent pain: targeted ultrasound; mammography if aged 40 or over, or with risk factors.
- Always consider musculoskeletal and other extramammary causes. Beware inflammatory carcinoma if erythema or skin change accompanies pain.

**Infection and inflammation:**
- Ultrasound confirms abscess formation and guides aspiration or drain placement.
- Underlying malignancy must be excluded in non-lactational mastitis, particularly in smokers (who are prone to periductal mastitis) and in cases that fail to resolve with antibiotics.
- Inflammatory carcinoma requires urgent imaging and simultaneous skin and breast core biopsy.

**Nipple and areolar changes:**
- Paget's disease presents as unilateral eczematous change of the nipple itself (not just the areola). Mammography and ultrasound should be performed to identify underlying DCIS or invasive disease; biopsy the nipple lesion.
- New nipple inversion requires full imaging; compare with any prior photographs or imaging.

**Pregnancy and lactation:**
- Ultrasound is first-line. Mammography is not contraindicated if clinically indicated; dose is low and fetal gonadal exposure negligible with appropriate shielding. Breast density is high in lactation, limiting sensitivity.
- Common entities include galactocele (fat–fluid level), lactational adenoma, and mastitis. Cancer can occur during pregnancy and lactation — do not dismiss a suspicious solid mass because of the clinical context.

## Mammographic Appearances in Detail

### Masses

The mammographic assessment of a mass rests primarily on margin characterisation:

- **Circumscribed**: clear, abrupt transition to surrounding tissue — favours benign. If fully circumscribed in all projections, oval or round, and stable or of long duration, the risk of malignancy is low.
- **Obscured**: margin hidden by overlying tissue — resolve with compression or DBT before assigning significance.
- **Indistinct**: no clear demarcation — indeterminate; requires further evaluation.
- **Microlobulated**: small undulations along the margin — intermediate suspicion.
- **Spiculated**: radiating lines extending from the mass — high suspicion; desmoplastic reaction is the pathological correlate.
