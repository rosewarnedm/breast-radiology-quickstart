# Chapter 12: Case-Based Learning

## How to use this chapter

This chapter presents a series of realistic cases from UK breast practice to teach underlying principles rather than isolated facts. Each case traces the pathway from presentation through imaging, interpretation, triple assessment, biopsy, and management. The aim is to show how appearances arise from anatomy and pathology, why certain findings are benign or suspicious, and how multimodality correlation reduces diagnostic uncertainty.

All cases are anchored to the NHS Breast Screening Programme (NHSBSP) and symptomatic services:

- The NHSBSP invites women aged 50–70 for two-view digital mammography every three years, with double reading and arbitration. Extension to 47–73 has been trialled in some areas; local practice varies.
- Assessment after recall follows **triple assessment**: clinical examination (P1–P5), mammography (M1–M5), ultrasound (U1–U5), and image-guided biopsy with B-categorisation (B1–B5). Diagnosis rests on **concordance** between all three limbs.
- Digital mammography is the screening standard. Digital breast tomosynthesis (DBT) is widely used at assessment. Ultrasound is indispensable for characterising masses and guiding biopsy. MRI is used selectively for problem-solving, occult disease, and preoperative staging.

Each case uses a consistent structure: clinical context → imaging findings → reasoning and differential → UK 5-point scores → biopsy and pathology → pitfalls and pearls → key learning points. Where practice legitimately varies, options are stated with rationale.

## Case 12.1: Screen-detected spiculated mass — invasive carcinoma

### Clinical context
56-year-old woman, routine NHSBSP, asymptomatic. Both readers recall for a left upper outer quadrant lesion; arbitration not required.

### Imaging findings

**Mammography:** Irregular spiculated mass at 10 o'clock, posterior third, approximately 12 mm. High density relative to adjacent fibroglandular tissue. Fine radiating spicules tether Cooper's ligaments; subtle focal architectural distortion; no calcifications; possible minimal skin retraction. Background density: scattered.

**DBT at assessment:** Confirms a discrete irregular mass with long spicules and separates it from overlapping tissue; no satellite lesions identified.

**Ultrasound:** 11 mm hypoechoic irregular mass, non-parallel orientation, angular margins, posterior acoustic shadowing, and an echogenic halo. Internal and rim vascularity on colour Doppler. One level I axillary node with mild eccentric cortical thickening (3.5 mm) but preserved fatty hilum.

### Reasoning and differential diagnosis

Spiculation reflects tumour-induced **desmoplasia**: the cancer stimulates a fibrotic stromal response that retracts Cooper's ligaments toward the lesion, producing radiating lines on the mammogram and posterior shadowing on ultrasound. This is a highly specific malignant signature.

The differential is narrow: **invasive carcinoma of no special type (NST)** versus **radial scar/complex sclerosing lesion (CSL)**. Both can produce spiculation and posterior shadowing, but several features favour carcinoma here:

- A discrete dense mass centre rather than pure distortion
- Internal vascularity on Doppler
- No central radiolucency or "black star" morphology on DBT
- Patient age

### UK scoring and management

- **P1** (asymptomatic), **M5** (malignant), **U5** (malignant)
- Biopsy: ultrasound-guided 14G core, 3–4 cores, with clip placement
- Axillary node: the threshold for targeted core biopsy varies by unit — some biopsy focal cortical thickening ≥3 mm, others ≥4 mm; follow local protocol

### Pathology

B5b invasive carcinoma NST, grade 2, ER positive, PR positive, HER2 negative. Node biopsy B2 (benign), or negative on surgical histology.

### Pitfalls and pearls

- Posterior third masses can be occult on one view; always interrogate both projections and use DBT
- If DBT features are equivocal between radial scar and carcinoma, vacuum-assisted biopsy (VAB) provides larger cores and reduces undersampling
- Small spiculated cancers in low-density breasts are easy to overlook among normal ligaments — search actively

### Key learning points

- Spiculation arises from desmoplasia tethering breast stroma; understanding this mechanism predicts the appearance across modalities
- DBT confirms a true mass and reduces false-positive recalls caused by tissue summation
- Correlate B result with M/U features; a B2 result in an M5/U5 lesion is **discordant** and requires re-biopsy or surgical excision

## Case 12.2: Focal asymmetry — avoiding over-recall

### Clinical context
52-year-old woman, routine screening. First reader recalls for new focal asymmetry on the right CC view; second reader considers summation artefact. Arbitration recommends assessment.

### Imaging findings

**Mammography:** Triangular focal asymmetry in the right retroareolar region, middle third, no defined borders, no associated distortion or calcifications. No corresponding abnormality on the MLO. No prior images available (prevalent round).

**Problem-solving views:** Spot compression CC disperses the asymmetry. Rolled CC views shift the density relative to skin markers, consistent with tissue summation.

**DBT:** Shows interdigitating fibroglandular tissue in distinct anatomical planes; no discrete mass or architectural distortion.

**Ultrasound:** Normal echotexture at the target area; ducts <2 mm, compressible; no focal lesion.

### Reasoning and differential diagnosis

A **focal asymmetry on a single view** that disperses on spot compression and shifts with rolled views is typical of summation artefact. The retroareolar region is particularly prone to overlap of ducts, fibrous tissue, and fat. Crucially, there is no correlate on the orthogonal view, on DBT, or on ultrasound. The absence of distortion, calcifications, or any mass characteristics supports a benign conclusion.

The key questions to ask with any focal asymmetry: Does it persist on compression? Is there a correlate on the orthogonal view? Does DBT show a real lesion? If all three answers are no, reassurance is appropriate.

### UK scoring and management

- **P1**, **M2** (benign), **U1** (normal)
- Outcome: reassurance and return to routine recall

### Pitfalls and pearls

- A single-view asymmetry that **persists** on spot compression or has any associated distortion requires further work-up regardless of the second reader's opinion
- Prior mammograms are invaluable: stability over several screening rounds strongly supports benignity; a **new** asymmetry warrants greater scrutiny even if imaging features appear benign
- Rolled views are a simple and underused tool — density that moves relative to skin markers indicates tissue overlap

### Key learning points

- Summation artefact is a common source of unnecessary recall; problem-solving views and DBT are the solution
- Assign M2/U1 confidently when all features, problem-solving views, and ultrasound are concordantly benign — over-investigation is not without harm

## Case 12.3: Calcifications — milk of calcium vs DCIS

### Clinical context
60-year-old woman, routine screening, recalled for calcifications in the left lower inner quadrant.

### Imaging findings

**Mammography (standard views):** Segmental distribution of microcalcifications spanning approximately 25 mm, middle third. On CC and MLO, the morphology appears smudgy, punctate to amorphous.

**Magnification views:**
- CC magnification: smudgy, amorphous, some punctate forms
- True lateral (ML) magnification: crescentic "teacup" shapes with dependent layering within microcysts — the morphology **changes between projections**

**DBT:** Clustered microcysts with dependent material; no associated mass or distortion.

**Ultrasound:** Scattered microcysts with echogenic foci and comet-tail artefact; no ductal ectasia or solid mass. (Note: ultrasound is generally insensitive for microcalcifications and is not the primary diagnostic tool here.)

### Reasoning and differential diagnosis

**Milk of calcium** forms when calcium salts sediment into fluid within microcysts. The key discriminating feature is **shape change across projections due to gravity**: teacup or crescent on the true lateral (where calcium layers dependently), and smudgy or amorphous on the CC (where the cyst is viewed end-on). This behaviour is pathognomonic for a benign process.

The important differential is **low-grade DCIS**, which can produce amorphous calcifications with a segmental distribution. However, DCIS calcifications do **not** change shape across projections and maintain their morphology regardless of beam angle. When classic layering is confirmed, reassurance is appropriate.

If any pleomorphic or fine linear/branching forms are present — even in the same cluster — the appearances must be upgraded to M4/M5 and tissue diagnosis sought.

### UK scoring and management

- **P1**, **M2** (after magnification lateral confirms layering), **U1–U2**
- Outcome: reassurance and return to routine recall

### Pitfalls and pearls

- A **true lateral (ML) view** is essential — do not rely on the CC alone to characterise calcifications; teacups are invisible end-on
- Mixed clusters can occur: milk of calcium may coexist with genuine DCIS in the same region; target the **most suspicious morphology** for biopsy
- In dense breasts, increase exposure settings and use magnification views routinely

### Key learning points

- Projectional shape change is the single most important discriminant for milk of calcium; understanding the gravitational mechanism makes this unforgettable
- Distribution modulates your index of suspicion, but morphology and cross-view behaviour drive the decision

## Case 12.4: Architectural distortion — radial scar vs carcinoma

### Clinical context
58-year-old woman, screen recall for subtle distortion in the right upper inner quadrant.

### Imaging findings

**Mammography:** Subtle central radiolucency with fine radiating lines and focal distortion; no mass centre; no calcifications. Best appreciated on the CC view; present but faint on MLO.

**DBT:** "Black star" morphology — radiolucent centre with long straight spicules radiating outward without a measurable mass. No discrete lesion to characterise further.

**Ultrasound:** Equivocal — vague shadowing without a definable mass; normal ducts; no vascular focus identified.

### Reasoning and differential diagnosis

**Architectural distortion without a mass** and with a central radiolucency is the classic appearance of a **radial scar/complex sclerosing lesion (CSL)**. These lesions arise from elastotic stroma with entrapped and proliferating ducts, simulating malignancy by distorting the normal ductal architecture. The imaging overlap with carcinoma is real and clinically important:

| Feature | Radial scar | Invasive carcinoma |
|---|---|---|
| Mass centre | Usually absent | Often present |
| Spicule character | Long, thin, straight | Shorter, thicker |
| Central density | Radiolucent | Usually dense |
| Associated calcifications | Uncommon | More common |

However, these are tendencies rather than rules, and a significant minority of radial scars harbour associated atypia (atypical ductal hyperplasia, lobular neoplasia) or even malignancy within or adjacent to them. **Tissue diagnosis is therefore mandatory for any architectural distortion.**

The negative ultrasound should not be reassuring here — invasive lobular carcinoma and radial scars are frequently sonographically occult.

### UK scoring and management

- **P1**, **M4** (suspicious), **U3** (indeterminate)
- Preferred biopsy: **stereotactic or DBT-guided VAB (8–10G)** to obtain adequate tissue and reduce the risk of underestimating higher-risk pathology; 14G core alone may miss atypia within the sclerosing lesion
- Clip placement and post-biopsy mammogram to confirm targeting

### Pathology

B3 complex sclerosing lesion without atypia.

Management of B3 CSL varies by unit and is evolving:
- **Vacuum-assisted excision (VAE)** to remove the radiological target with subsequent clip-site mammographic confirmation and surveillance — increasingly favoured
- **Surgical excision** if atypia is present (ADH, lobular neoplasia) or if local policy requires it

### Pitfalls and pearls

- Ultrasound-negative distortion still requires tissue diagnosis — do not let a negative scan provide false reassurance
- Ensure accurate targeting of the distortion **centre** on DBT; peripheral sampling may miss the atypia that determines management
- The post-biopsy mammogram is not optional: confirm the clip is at the site of distortion

### Key learning points

- Architectural distortion without a mass requires tissue diagnosis because the imaging overlap between radial scar and carcinoma is irresolvable by imaging alone
- DBT-guided VAB improves diagnostic accuracy and may be therapeutic for selected B3 lesions without atypia

## Case 12.5: Ill-defined increased density — invasive lobular carcinoma

### Clinical context
64-year-old woman, symptomatic clinic with vague thickening in the left upper outer quadrant. Clinical assessment: **P3** (indeterminate thickening).

### Imaging findings

**Mammography:** Subtle asymmetric density and architectural distortion without a discrete mass; mild nipple retraction. Scattered punctate calcifications not clearly associated. Background density: scattered to heterogeneously dense.

**DBT:** Improved delineation of distortion and subtle linear spicules along Cooper's ligaments; no clear measurable mass centre.

**Ultrasound:** Ill-defined hypoechoic area with posterior shadowing and indistinct margins over approximately 25 mm; not a circumscribed mass. Normal-appearing axillary nodes.

**MRI (extent problem-solving):** Regional clumped non-mass enhancement with segmental distribution; persistent-to-plateau kinetics; overall extent 38 mm. Possible additional contralateral focus of 6 mm.

### Reasoning and differential diagnosis

**Invasive lobular carcinoma (ILC)** grows in a single-file infiltrative pattern with minimal desmoplasia. This is why it produces diffuse thickening and distortion rather than a focal mass, and why it is consistently underestimated — or even occult — on both mammography and ultrasound. The key teaching point is that ILC looks like "not very much" on standard imaging, yet can be extensive at histology.

Differentials include radial scar/CSL, focal fibrosis, and post-traumatic scarring. MRI non-mass enhancement is not specific to ILC and can be seen in DCIS and areas of florid background parenchymal enhancement (particularly relevant in women on HRT).

### UK scoring and management

- **P3**, **M4**, **U4**; overall suspicion high — biopsy required
- Ultrasound-guided cores from the hypoechoic area; if ultrasound-guided sampling is non-diagnostic and a DBT target is visible, proceed to DBT-guided VAB; MRI-guided biopsy for the contralateral focus only if confirmation would alter management
- Clip placement essential for surgical planning

### Pathology

B5b classic invasive lobular carcinoma, grade 2, ER positive, HER2 negative.

### Pitfalls and pearls

- A "normal" or equivocal ultrasound in the context of a suspicious clinical finding and abnormal mammogram/DBT is **not** reassuring — proceed to biopsy
- ILC is consistently underestimated in extent on standard imaging; MRI is currently the best tool for extent mapping, but it too can overestimate — interpret in full clinical context before committing to mastectomy
- In peri- and postmenopausal women on HRT, background parenchymal enhancement on MRI can be prominent; this should be acknowledged when reporting

### Key learning points

- ILC's single-file growth pattern explains its subtle imaging features; maintain a low threshold for biopsy of any persistent, unexplained thickening
- MRI is valuable for extent assessment in ILC when standard imaging underestimates disease, but it is not the first-line investigation
