# Chapter 1: Breast Anatomy and Imaging Appearance

## Why anatomy matters in breast imaging

Breast imaging is an exercise in pattern recognition grounded in anatomy. Most benign and malignant processes arise from, expand within, or distort specific anatomical units, and understanding those units allows you to predict appearances, avoid pitfalls, and reason through unfamiliar cases rather than simply memorising lists.

Four principles underpin everything that follows.

First, most clinically important pathology — both benign and malignant — originates in the **terminal duct lobular unit (TDLU)**. The morphology of a mass, the pattern of calcifications, and the distribution of enhancement on MRI often directly mirror the underlying duct–lobular architecture. Once you internalise that geometry, many appearances become predictable rather than arbitrary.

Second, descriptive terms such as "dense" or "echogenic" are **relative**. Always anchor your description to the surrounding reference tissue on that modality and on that individual study.

Third, each modality interrogates the breast differently. **Projection and superimposition** dominate mammography. **Reflectivity and beam–tissue interaction** dominate ultrasound. **Contrast kinetics and background parenchymal enhancement** dominate MRI. The same lesion can therefore look strikingly different across modalities — that is not a failing of the technique but a feature you can exploit.

Fourth, in UK practice imaging is never standalone. Every finding must be integrated within **triple assessment** and expressed as a scored opinion (M or U) that sits alongside the clinical (P) and pathology (B) scores to drive a joint management decision.

## The UK framework: screening and assessment

### The NHS Breast Screening Programme

Women aged 50–70 are routinely invited every three years for **two-view mammography** (mediolateral oblique and craniocaudal). Women over 70 may self-refer. Age-extension trials have operated in various regions; know your unit's current policy, as this affects the population you will see in the screening reading room.

All screens are **independently double-read**, with arbitration for discordant cases. The outcome is binary: return to routine screening, or recall to an assessment clinic.

### The symptomatic and assessment pathway

**Triple assessment** combines:
- **P score** (clinical examination): P1 normal → P5 malignant
- **M score** (mammographic impression): M1 normal → M5 malignant
- **U score** (ultrasound impression): U1 normal → U5 malignant
- **B score** (core biopsy histology): B1 normal tissue → B2 benign → B3 lesion of uncertain malignant potential → B4 suspicious → B5 malignant (qualified as in situ or invasive)

The power of this system lies in the **concordance check**. A B2 result that does not plausibly explain an M4 or U4 finding demands re-biopsy or surgical discussion — the imaging drives that expectation, not the other way round. You must be able to justify your score with clear reasoning, not just assign a number.

## Macro- and microanatomy of the breast

### Surface structures and compartments

The breast is bounded superficially by **skin** (including the nipple–areolar complex, which contains multiple duct orifices and Montgomery glands in the areola) and supported by **Cooper's ligaments** — fibrous condensations running from the dermis through the subcutaneous fat to the pectoral fascia. These ligaments are crucial: spiculated cancers and radial scars deform them, producing the architectural distortion and skin retraction you will learn to seek.

Deep to the skin lies **subcutaneous fat**, then the **fibroglandular tissue** proper — approximately 15–20 lobes arranged radially around the nipple–areolar complex, each subdividing into segmental ducts, lobules, and ultimately TDLUs. Density is greatest superiorly and laterally in most women, extending into the **axillary tail of Spence**. Posterior to the fibroglandular tissue is a **retromammary fat plane** that allows the breast to move over the pectoralis fascia.

### Vascular and lymphatic pathways

The breast receives its blood supply from the **internal mammary artery** medially and the **lateral thoracic artery** laterally. Superficial veins drain toward the axilla and internal mammary chain; prominent vascularity is normal during lactation and should not be mistaken for pathological hypervascularisation.

Lymphatic drainage is predominantly to **level I–III axillary nodes**, with secondary routes to the internal mammary and supraclavicular nodes. **Intramammary lymph nodes** — normal nodes residing within the breast parenchyma, most commonly in the upper outer quadrant — are a normal finding you must be able to recognise confidently on both mammography and ultrasound.

### Microanatomy: the TDLU and why it matters

The TDLU consists of a terminal duct and its cluster of associated acini (lobules). It is the origin of most breast carcinomas — both ductal and lobular — and of many benign proliferative lesions including fibrocystic change, fibroadenomas, and adenosis.

The **duct–lobular geometry** directly shapes imaging appearances:
- Processes that spread along ducts toward the nipple produce **segmental or linear distributions** — the hallmark of DCIS on mammography.
- Processes that remain lobulocentric produce **grouped or clustered distributions**.

Once you recognise this, the morphology of a calcification cluster or a non-mass enhancement pattern becomes a clue to the underlying pathological process, not an arbitrary descriptor.

### Physiological variation through life

The breast is a dynamic organ, and its imaging appearance changes substantially with age and hormonal status.

- **Reproductive years**: fibroglandular tissue is relatively dense, TDLUs prominent. Mammographic sensitivity is reduced in younger women; ultrasound becomes more important.
- **Pregnancy and lactation**: lobuloalveolar proliferation, duct dilatation, increased vascularity, and higher water content all increase density. Ultrasound is the primary modality in symptomatic pregnant or lactating women, but suspicious features still demand biopsy — pregnancy-associated breast cancer is rare but real.
- **Perimenopause and beyond**: TDLUs involute and are replaced by fat. Involution is heterogeneous and patchy, which explains why mammographic density remains varied even in older women. Increasing fat content generally improves mammographic sensitivity.
- **Hormone replacement therapy**: can increase both mammographic density and MRI background parenchymal enhancement, potentially reducing sensitivity. Note this when comparing prior studies.
- **Male breast**: predominantly fatty with a subareolar ductal remnant. Gynaecomastia reflects ductal proliferation with stromal oedema, and must be distinguished from the rare male breast cancer.

## From anatomy to mammographic appearance

### Physics in brief

Mammography measures **differential x-ray attenuation**. Fat is radiolucent (appears dark); fibroglandular stroma, tumour, and calcified or fibrotic tissues attenuate more (appear white). Because mammography is a **projection technique**, structures summate — this creates apparent findings that do not exist and obscures real ones. Much of practical mammographic skill lies in managing superimposition.

### Standard positioning and its consequences

**MLO (mediolateral oblique)**: aimed to include the greatest tissue volume, with the pectoralis muscle visible down to at least the level of the nipple and the inframammary angle demonstrated. Includes the axillary tail.

This view captures the greatest tissue volume, including the axillary tail.*

**CC (craniocaudal)**: complements the MLO by profiling medial and lateral tissue differently. The **posterior nipple line** on CC should be within 1 cm of that on the MLO; a larger discrepancy suggests posterior tissue has been excluded and a cancer could have been missed.

Positioning quality directly determines sensitivity. The commonest location for a missed cancer is the posterior upper outer quadrant — a consequence of inadequate axillary tail inclusion on the MLO. When reviewing a mammogram, always check posterior nipple line, pectoral muscle level, inframammary angle, and axillary tail before interpreting the parenchyma.

**Additional projections** — ML/LM, XCCL, spot compression, magnification, rolled views — are workhorses of the assessment clinic. They help localise a finding in three dimensions, resolve superimposition, and characterise calcification morphology. Know when to request them and what question each answers.

### Normal mammographic landmarks

- **Skin**: thin and uniform. Focal thickening suggests oedema, inflammation, or tumour infiltration. Global thickening occurs with cardiac or renal failure and after radiotherapy.
- **Nipple**: soft-tissue density. Apparent subareolar mass can simply be a nipple projected en face — use a tangential view or skin marker to confirm.
- **Pectoralis shadow**: triangular density with a convex anterior border on the MLO.
- **Vessels**: serpiginous linear densities; tram-track calcification with age or diabetes is benign.
- **Cooper's ligaments**: fine curvilinear lines converging on the nipple. Disruption manifests as architectural distortion or skin tethering.
- **Intramammary lymph node**: oval or reniform, circumscribed, with a **central lucent hilum**. Typically less than 1 cm. Stability and hilar lucency are the reassuring features.

### Parenchymal density

UK units broadly use a four-category system analogous to BI-RADS density:

| Category | Description |
|---|---|
| A | Almost entirely fatty |
| B | Scattered areas of fibroglandular tissue |
| C | Heterogeneously dense |
| D | Extremely dense |

Reporting density matters because it sets expectations: in a dense breast, a non-calcified cancer of any size may simply not be visible on mammography. This is not a reporting error but a fundamental limitation of the technique, and it drives the increasing use of **digital breast tomosynthesis** and supplementary ultrasound. Some units routinely inform women of their density category for this reason.

### The four mammographic findings: masses, asymmetries, architectural distortion, and calcifications

These are not merely descriptive categories — each reflects a different interaction between the pathological process and the surrounding tissue.

**Masses**

A true mass is seen on **two orthogonal views**. Describe:
- *Shape*: round or oval suggests benignity; irregular raises suspicion.
- *Margin*: circumscribed favours benign; microlobulated or obscured is indeterminate; spiculated is highly suspicious.
- *Density relative to fibroglandular tissue*: fat-containing masses (lipoma, oil cyst, hamartoma) are radiolucent and virtually always benign. Most carcinomas are isodense or hyperdense; a hypodense circumscribed mass that is not fat-containing may be a mucinous carcinoma or other less common entity.
- *Associated features*: skin or nipple retraction, trabecular thickening, satellite calcifications.

**Asymmetries**

A **focal asymmetry** is a density visible on two views but lacking definable margins — often a summation artefact, but persistent or developing asymmetry warrants workup. A **developing asymmetry** (new or enlarging compared to prior) carries a meaningfully higher positive predictive value and should prompt recall.

**Architectural distortion**

Lines and spiculations radiating from a point without an obvious central mass. The mechanism is desmoplastic reaction aligning with Cooper's ligaments. The differential is critical: invasive carcinoma vs. radial scar/complex sclerosing lesion vs. post-surgical change. Tomosynthesis substantially improves detection and localisation; most architectural distortions at assessment will require biopsy.

**Calcifications**

The key principle is that **morphology and distribution together determine risk** — neither alone is sufficient.

*Morphology*:
- Benign patterns: skin calcifications (dermal, may show lucent centre — use tangential view to confirm), vascular (tram-track), coarse or "popcorn" (involuting fibroadenoma), large dystrophic with lucent centres, eggshell (fat necrosis), **milk of calcium** (curvilinear on CC; layering and cup-shaped on the true lateral ML — always obtain a lateral view when you suspect this, as it converts an indeterminate appearance into a confidently benign one).
- Suspicious morphologies: **fine pleomorphic** and **fine linear/branching** calcifications.

*Distribution* (from lower to higher concern):
- Diffuse/scattered: typically benign, especially if bilateral and symmetrical.
- Regional: large volume not conforming to a duct; indeterminate; context matters.
- Grouped/clustered: several calcifications within 1–2 cm; risk depends heavily on morphology.
- **Linear or segmental**: aligned along a ductal tree; think DCIS until proven otherwise.

A practical note: **tomosynthesis** is not a substitute for **magnification views** when characterising microcalcification morphology. Magnification 2D remains the gold standard for fine detail; tomosynthesis helps with distribution and associated mass or distortion.

## Ultrasound appearance by layer and structure

Ultrasound is operator-dependent and reflective. Echogenicity is always **relative** — most breast ultrasound protocols use fat as the reference; clarify this when describing findings, as system presets vary.

### Normal layers

- **Skin**: echogenic bilaminar line, approximately 2 mm thick. Cobblestoning (thickening with increased echogenicity of the subcutaneous layer) indicates oedema — seen in mastitis, inflammatory carcinoma, and lymphoedema.
- **Subcutaneous fat**: hypoechoic lobules separated by thin echogenic septa (Cooper's ligaments).
- **Fibroglandular tissue**: heterogeneous, typically hyperechoic relative to fat; more prominent in younger women.
- **Ducts**: anechoic tubular structures radiating from the nipple–areolar complex; best seen in the **radial plane**. Mild dilatation is common perimenopausal and does not in isolation require biopsy.
- **Pectoralis muscle**: hypoechoic and striated, deep to the breast. Ribs are curved hyperechoic structures with posterior acoustic shadowing — do not mistake them for masses.
- **Intramammary lymph node**: oval or reniform; hypoechoic cortex (≤3 mm, concentric); echogenic fatty hilum; hilar vascularity on Doppler.

### Describing a mass on ultrasound

Standard descriptors — **shape, orientation, margin, echo pattern, posterior features, surrounding tissue changes, calcifications, and vascularity** — are not bureaucratic box-ticking; each reflects the underlying growth behaviour.

*Features favouring benignity*: oval shape, circumscribed margins, parallel orientation (wider than tall), homogeneous echotexture, posterior acoustic enhancement, thin echogenic pseudocapsule.

*Features raising suspicion*: irregular or angular margins, **non-parallel orientation** ("taller than wide" — the classic teaching, though use judgement; a small lobulated fibroadenoma oriented slightly obliquely is not automatically suspicious), heterogeneous internal echotexture, posterior acoustic shadowing, echogenic halo (desmoplasia), architectural distortion in surrounding tissue, associated duct changes.

### Scanning technique

Always scan in **radial and anti-radial planes** around the nipple. This leverages ductal anatomy — lesions arising in a duct will be most conspicuous in the plane aligned with it — and helps detect subtle distortion aligned with Cooper's ligaments. Scan the **axilla** routinely: intramammary nodes, metastatic axillary nodes, and occasionally primary cancers in the axillary tail are found here.

## MRI appearance and what it tells you

MRI is the most sensitive modality for breast cancer detection but has lower specificity. Interpret it with that trade-off consciously in mind.

### Background parenchymal enhancement and fibroglandular tissue

**Fibroglandular tissue amount** (minimal, mild, moderate, marked) is analogous to mammographic density but not directly interchangeable. **Background parenchymal enhancement (BPE)** — the baseline enhancement of normal glandular tissue — is graded minimal, mild, moderate, or marked. High BPE can mask lesions and generate false positives. It increases in the luteal phase of the menstrual cycle, with HRT, and during pregnancy and lactation. Where feasible, schedule MRI in the **follicular phase** (days 7–14); in UK practice this is not always achievable, so note the cycle phase and interpret BPE accordingly.

### Tissue signal characteristics

- **T1-weighted**: fat is bright; glandular tissue is intermediate signal; haemorrhage and proteinaceous fluid are bright (useful in characterising complex lesions).
- **T2-weighted**: simple cysts and oedema are bright; fibrous tissue is dark. A markedly T2-bright mass (fibroadenoma, mucinous carcinoma, cyst) has a different differential than a T2-dark spiculated mass (most invasive carcinomas).
- **Dynamic contrast enhancement**: malignancy tends to enhance rapidly
