# Chapter 5: Normal Variants and Benign Findings

## Overview and UK Context

Recognising benign breast findings confidently is one of the most practically important skills in breast radiology. Get it right and you reduce unnecessary recalls, biopsies, and patient anxiety whilst maintaining cancer detection rates. Get it wrong in the other direction — over-reassuring — and cancers are missed. This tension sits at the heart of both the NHS Breast Screening Programme (NHSBSP) and symptomatic triple assessment.

**The NHSBSP** invites women aged 50–70 for two-view mammography every three years, with some units extending this range locally. Double reading with arbitration is standard, and most screen-detected abnormalities prove benign on assessment. Your job at the reading light box is to separate the small proportion of true positives from an overwhelming background of normal and benign appearances.

**Triple assessment** integrates clinical examination (P score), imaging (M for mammography, U for ultrasound, with tomosynthesis and MRI as adjuncts), and tissue diagnosis (B score). Each is scored 1–5: 1 = normal, 2 = benign, 3 = indeterminate, 4 = suspicious, 5 = malignant. A concordant M2/U2/B2 allows safe discharge; discordance at any level should prompt reconsideration at the multidisciplinary team (MDT). Understanding where each benign entity sits within this framework — and why — is what this chapter is about.

## Foundational Principles

Before cataloguing individual entities, internalise these principles. They explain the imaging rather than asking you to memorise lists.

**Form follows histology.** Fat is radiolucent; fibrous and epithelial tissue attenuates X-rays and appears dense. Benign processes typically expand or compress surrounding structures whilst respecting lobular architecture. Malignancy infiltrates and provokes a desmoplastic stromal reaction, pulling tissue inwards and producing the spiculation and architectural distortion you learn to fear.

**Margins matter more than size.** A smooth, circumscribed margin indicates a pushing, expansile growth — almost always benign or low-grade. Angular, indistinct, or spiculated margins reflect infiltrative behaviour. A 5 mm spiculated mass is more worrying than a 3 cm circumscribed one.

**Calcification interpretation is about morphology and distribution together.** Coarse, lucent-centred, rim, vascular, and rod-like calcifications are reliably benign. Fine pleomorphic or linear/branching calcifications — particularly in a segmental distribution tracking a ductal tree — raise serious concern for ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS). Never interpret morphology in isolation from distribution, and vice versa.

**Distribution tells a biological story.** Diffuse, bilateral, scattered changes overwhelmingly favour benignity. Focal, segmental, or linear changes aligned with a ductal territory demand explanation.

**Multimodality correlation resolves ambiguity.** Tomosynthesis separates genuine masses from summation artefact. Ultrasound distinguishes cystic from solid. MRI solves the occasional problem that other modalities cannot — but use it selectively and purposefully.

**Priors are underused.** Stability of a finding over two or more screening rounds powerfully supports benignity. Conversely, interval growth or change in morphology should prompt reassessment even of an apparently benign lesion.

## Normal Anatomy and Variants that Mimic Disease

### Parenchymal Pattern and Breast Density

Fibroglandular tissue involutes with age and parity and increases with hormone replacement therapy (HRT). Higher density reduces mammographic sensitivity — a fact you must communicate in your report where relevant — and generates summation artefacts that mimic genuine masses or asymmetries. This is the single commonest cause of screening recall.

A one-view focal asymmetry that disappears on the orthogonal view, disperses on spot compression or tomosynthesis, and has no ultrasound correlate is summation: score M2 and discharge. If it persists on two views without suspicious features, it is M3 and warrants targeted ultrasound and tomosynthesis before a biopsy decision. Develop a systematic habit: always check the orthogonal view before worrying about a unilateral density.

### Nipple and Areolar Region

Long-standing bilateral nipple inversion is a normal variant. New, unilateral inversion associated with subareolar mass or skin tethering is a red flag. Montgomery glands (sebaceous glands around the areola) and mild ductal ectasia produce benign periareolar nodularity and retroareolar tubular densities that can alarm the unwary. When the position of a finding on the MLO view is uncertain, a nipple marker will confirm or exclude the nipple as the culprit.

### Axillary Tail and Accessory Tissue

The axillary tail varies in size and is often asymmetrical — this is normal. True accessory breast tissue in the axilla or inframammary fold behaves like normal parenchyma and is subject to the same benign and malignant processes. Do not mistake prominent axillary tail for pathological adenopathy without ultrasound assessment.

Intramammary lymph nodes (IMLNs) are common in the upper outer quadrant. Classic features — reniform shape, lucent fatty hilum, less than 10–12 mm — are M2/U2. An IMLN without a demonstrable fatty hilum on ultrasound deserves closer scrutiny.

### Chest Wall Structures

Pectoralis major and serratus anterior may be partially imaged and mimic a posterior mass; their triangular outline on the MLO and consistency across views gives them away. The sternalis muscle — a parasternal vertical sheet of muscle present in a minority of individuals — appears as a stable anterior medial density on CC views. Recognise it to avoid recall.

Tortuous vessels and vascular calcifications are benign. If you see tram-track calcifications in a medial or lower location, call them vascular and move on.

### Skin, Dermis, and External Artefacts

Naevi, seborrhoeic keratoses, and dermal lesions produce well-circumscribed, often lucent-centred densities that can be mistaken for intramammary masses. The "tattoo sign" — a lesion that is visible on the skin image and moves with the skin — and tangential views confirm dermal origin. **Always use skin markers for moles, scars, and palpable lesions before imaging.** This simple step prevents a significant proportion of unnecessary recalls.

Skin folds produce linear densities and edge effects that mimic distortion. Deodorant and talcum powder create calcification-like artefacts; if you suspect this, ask the radiographer to wipe the skin and repeat. Hair braids, ECG electrodes, and jewellery produce geometric, high-density artefacts that are visually distinctive — check the skin images and reposition.

## Technical and Interpretive Pitfalls

These are the avoidable errors that generate unnecessary patient recall and anxiety:

- **Summation density** recalled as a mass — resolve with tomosynthesis, rolled views, spot compression, and comparison with priors
- **Deodorant or powder** misread as microcalcifications — wipe and repeat
- **Skin lesions without markers** recalled as intramammary masses — mark before imaging
- **Inadequate positioning** — missing posterior tissue on CC or absent pectoral muscle on MLO may obscure posterior lesions or create pseudo-masses; document positioning quality and repeat if suboptimal
- **Insufficient use of priors** — always retrieve and compare previous examinations before scoring

## The UK 5-Point Scoring System in Practice

The scoring system is a communication tool, not a rigid algorithm. Use it to convey your degree of certainty and to guide clinical decisions:

| Score | Meaning | Typical action |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| M/U 1 | Normal | No action |
| M/U 2 | Benign | Discharge (screening) or reassure (symptomatic) |
| M/U 3 | Indeterminate / possibly benign | Recall to assessment; consider biopsy |
| M/U 4 | Suspicious | Biopsy |
| M/U 5 | Malignant | Biopsy; oncological planning |
| B1 | Normal / insufficient | Repeat if needed |
| B2 | Benign | Concordance check; discharge if imaging concordant |
| B3 | Uncertain malignant potential | MDT discussion; often vacuum excision |
| B4 | Suspicious | Treat as malignant pending further tissue |
| B5 | Malignant | Treat |

The critical habit is **imaging–pathology concordance**. A B2 result is only reassuring if it explains the imaging finding. A B2 result for a core from an M4 mass is discordant and must be discussed at MDT — the biopsy may have missed the target.

## Cysts and Duct-Related Benignities

### Simple Cyst

Simple cysts arise from dilatation of terminal duct lobular units (TDLUs) and are the commonest benign breast mass. They are hormonally responsive and may fluctuate in size.

On **mammography**, they appear as round or oval, well-circumscribed, equal-density masses — often invisible in dense tissue. On **ultrasound**, the diagnosis is straightforward: anechoic internal content, imperceptible wall, posterior acoustic enhancement, no internal vascularity. This is U2. No biopsy is required. Aspirate only if the cyst is tense and symptomatic, or if there is genuine diagnostic uncertainty.

*Pitfall:* Peripheral calcification can make a cyst appear irregular or dense on mammography. Ultrasound immediately clarifies — do not biopsy a calcified cyst without ultrasound correlation.

### Complicated Cyst and Cluster of Microcysts

A **complicated cyst** contains low-level internal echoes (from protein, blood, or debris), may show fluid-debris levels, and has thin septa but no solid vascular component. This is U2–U3 depending on your confidence and the clinical context. It does not need biopsy if the appearances are classic and concordant with mammography.

A **cluster of microcysts** — grouped tiny anechoic foci with thin septations and no internal flow — often explains grouped amorphous or milk of calcium calcifications on mammography. If the correlation is convincing and the appearances are classic, short-interval follow-up or discharge is reasonable, though practice varies between units. When the calcifications are indeterminate and the cluster does not fully account for them, stereotactic biopsy is appropriate.

The key distinction is **complex cystic-solid mass**: thick irregular walls, internal vascular solid nodules, or thick septations with flow are not "complicated cysts" and require biopsy.

### Duct Ectasia and Periductal Change

Ductal ectasia — dilatation of subareolar ducts with inspissated secretions — is common in peri- and post-menopausal women. On mammography it produces tubular retroareolar densities and characteristically **rod-like or coarse calcifications radiating from the nipple**, often bilateral. On ultrasound, dilated ducts contain echogenic debris without a vascular intraductal mass.

Management is conservative in the absence of suspicious features. Unilateral bloody discharge or an intraductal vascular mass (suggesting papilloma) warrants further investigation.

*Pitfall:* Do not confuse rod-like secretory calcifications (benign, retroareolar, often bilateral) with linear/branching casting-type calcifications in a segmental distribution, which are characteristic of high-grade DCIS.

### Intraductal Papilloma

A central, solitary papilloma classically presents with nipple discharge — serous or blood-stained — and may be occult on mammography. Ultrasound shows a vascular intraductal soft-tissue mass within a dilated duct. Tissue diagnosis is standard because of the upgrade risk to atypia or malignancy, particularly with peripheral papillomas. A B2 result may be managed with surveillance or vacuum excision depending on local MDT policy — this is an area where practice legitimately varies.

## Fibroepithelial and Stromal Benign Masses

### Fibroadenoma

Fibroadenomas are the commonest benign solid mass, arising from stromal and epithelial proliferation of the TDLU. Their well-circumscribed pushing margins explain their imaging appearance.

On **mammography**: oval or round, circumscribed, equal or low density. Involuting lesions develop coarse "popcorn" calcifications that are pathognomonic — score M2 without further workup. On **ultrasound**: oval, parallel orientation (wider than tall), circumscribed, homogeneous hypoechoic or isoechoic, gently lobulated, avascular or with peripheral flow only. U2 in a classic case.

A classic fibroadenoma in a young woman (under 35–40, depending on unit policy) with typical imaging can be discharged without biopsy. **Biopsy if:** the lesion is new in a woman over 40, there are atypical features, there is rapid growth, or the clinical impression does not fit.

*Differentials to consider:*
- **Phyllodes tumour**: typically larger, faster growing, lobulated, with intracapsular cleft-like spaces. Biopsy if ≥3 cm, growing, or atypical — core biopsy may underestimate grade
- **Circumscribed carcinoma** (mucinous, medullary, metaplastic): new circumscribed masses in older women should not be assumed benign without biopsy

### Hamartoma (Fibroadenolipoma)

A hamartoma is a disorganised but entirely benign mixture of fat and glandular tissue enclosed within a thin pseudocapsule. The mammographic appearance is diagnostic: **"breast within a breast"** — interspersed fat and soft-tissue densities within a thin rim. Ultrasound shows a heterogeneous, compressible, well-circumscribed mass with internal fat echoes. Classic appearances are M2/U2; no biopsy required. Caution if fat is absent — a purely soft-tissue mass without the characteristic mixture warrants biopsy.

### Lipoma

A circumscribed, radiolucent mass with a thin capsule. Compressible and avascular on ultrasound. Reassure if classic.

### Pseudoangiomatous Stromal Hyperplasia (PASH)

PASH is a benign myofibroblastic proliferation forming slit-like stromal spaces. It presents as a circumscribed or partially obscured hypoechoic mass, often large and avascular. Core biopsy yields B2. Management is observation unless the lesion is symptomatic, enlarging rapidly, or causing diagnostic uncertainty. Do not be falsely reassured by benign histology if the mass is growing — interval growth should prompt MDT discussion.

### Focal Fibrosis and Sclerosing Adenosis

These represent benign proliferative changes within the lobular unit. Both can mimic carcinoma: sclerosing adenosis may produce grouped amorphous calcifications or architectural distortion on mammography, and marked posterior acoustic shadowing from an ill-defined hypoechoic area on ultrasound. Biopsy is usually required for diagnosis. A B2 result is concordant; imaging–pathology correlation at MDT is essential.

## Fat Necrosis and Post-Traumatic/Post-Surgical Change

Fat necrosis results from liquefaction and saponification of adipose tissue after trauma, surgery, or radiotherapy. Its imaging appearance evolves with time:

- **Early**: focal asymmetry, ill-defined mass, or architectural distortion — can closely mimic carcinoma
- **Established**: oil cyst (radiolucent centre) with thin eggshell rim calcification, or coarse dystrophic calcifications — pathognomonic (M2)

On ultrasound, fat necrosis produces complex cysts with echogenic internal fat, fat-fluid levels, mural nodules without vascularity, and echogenic bands. On MRI, a thin smooth enhancing rim is benign; thick, irregular rim enhancement in a scarred field warrants biopsy.

Classic oil cysts and eggshell calcifications are M2/U2 — discharge with confidence. However, **not all spiculation near a scar is fat necrosis**. Interval change, a new mass-like opacity, or malignant-type calcifications in a previously treated area must prompt biopsy, because local recurrence and new primary tumours occur in scarred tissue.
