# Chapter 8: Structured Reporting

## Why structured reporting matters

Structured reporting is not bureaucratic formality — it is a discipline that forces you to answer the clinical question clearly, describe findings reproducibly, assign a category, and state a plan. A beautifully descriptive report that ends without a category or recommendation has failed the patient and the referring team.

Done well, structured reporting:

- reduces omissions and ambiguity, particularly under time pressure
- enables meaningful comparison across modalities and with pathology
- supports double reading, arbitration, and MDT discussion
- underpins audit — recall positive predictive values, interval cancer reviews, and biopsy concordance rates — all of which the NHSBSP monitors closely
- helps you build a systematic habit that scales to unfamiliar or complex cases

In UK practice, your report must integrate with the **triple assessment pathway**: clinical P-score, mammographic M-score, ultrasound U-score, and histological B category. The goal is not description for its own sake but coherent decision-making — a single overall risk category and an actionable plan.

---

## Where structured reporting fits in the UK pathway

**Screening (NHSBSP):** Women aged 50–70 are invited three-yearly for two-view mammography with double reading. Reports must be concise, codeable, and communicate a clear recall or no-recall decision with a stated reason. Assessment clinic findings — targeted imaging, biopsy, and outcomes — are documented in the National Breast Screening System (NBSS). The emphasis is on throughput, reproducibility, and audit.

**Symptomatic (triple assessment):** The report must integrate with same-day clinical workflow, provide a category-based risk assessment, and state a management plan that can be enacted immediately. A P5 clinical finding demands escalation even if the mammogram appears normal — discordance must be named and resolved.

**Additional modalities:** Tomosynthesis (DBT) is used as a problem-solving tool or within assessment; MRI is reserved for specific indications (extent of disease in known cancer, occult primary, neoadjuvant response assessment, implant integrity); image-guided biopsy reports are an integral part of the structured pathway, not an afterthought.

## Principles of a good breast report

- **Start with the question.** Screening decision or symptomatic problem?
- **Be systematic.** Use the same order every time; omissions happen when you improvise.
- **Be selective.** Describe what changes management. Avoid hedging and exhaustive lists of normal findings.
- **Categorise explicitly.** Assign M and/or U scores to each relevant finding and state an overall category.
- **Correlate.** Across prior studies, across modalities, and with pathology. Always state whether imaging and histology are concordant.
- **Close the loop.** End with a recommendation and a timescale.

## The UK 5-point scoring systems

These categories encode probability and drive action. Use them precisely.

| Score | Clinical (P) | Mammography (M) / Ultrasound (U) | Histology (B) |
|-------|-------------|----------------------------------|---------------|
| 1 | Normal | Normal | Normal tissue / insufficient |
| 2 | Benign | Benign | Benign |
| 3 | Indeterminate | Indeterminate / probably benign | Uncertain malignant potential (B3) |
| 4 | Suspicious | Suspicious | Suspicious of malignancy |
| 5 | Malignant | Highly suspicious of malignancy | Malignant |

**B5** is commonly subdivided: **B5a** non-invasive (e.g., DCIS), **B5b** invasive carcinoma, with some centres noting microinvasion separately. Check what your laboratory uses and state it explicitly in your report — assumptions cost patients.

**B3 lesions** (lobular neoplasia, atypical ductal hyperplasia, flat epithelial atypia, papilloma, radial scar) warrant particular care: management varies by diagnosis and local MDT policy. Do not conflate B3 with B2; they carry meaningfully different upgrade rates.

Some centres apply a similar 1–5 scale to MRI (R1–R5). This is acceptable provided the logic and the consequent action are transparent.

## Core components of a structured breast imaging report

### 1) Administrative and clinical details

- Patient identifiers and laterality check — verify before you start.
- Indication: screening recall with reason stated, or symptom (side, clockface position, distance from nipple, duration).
- P-score if provided.
- Relevant history: age, menopausal and hormonal status, pregnancy or lactation, prior surgery, biopsy or radiotherapy, implants, high-risk status.

### 2) Prior comparison

State the dates and modalities of relevant prior studies, or note explicitly that none are available. Summarise the key change: new, increased, stable, or decreased. If stability is uncertain because of technical differences, say so — "probably stable but technically limited comparison" is honest and useful.

### 3) Technique and limitations

- **Mammography:** views performed, whether DBT was used, any magnification or spot views, positioning issues or implants requiring Eklund technique.
- **Ultrasound:** targeted versus whole-breast, transducer frequency, any adjuncts (elastography, contrast), and limitations (e.g., artefact from surgical scar).
- **MRI:** field strength, protocol, cycle timing in pre-menopausal women, contrast used, adequacy of fat suppression, motion artefact.
- **Biopsy:** guidance modality, needle gauge, vacuum assistance, local anaesthetic, clip type, specimen radiography result.

Documenting limitations is not defensive — it tells the clinician what the imaging can and cannot exclude.

### 4) Breast composition

Note parenchymal density where it affects sensitivity or informs the decision, for example: *"Heterogeneously dense parenchyma, which may limit detection of small lesions."* The NHSBSP does not currently mandate formal density notification to patients, but transparency in reporting is good practice.

### 5) Systematic description of findings

Work through the same order every time:

**Skin and nipple-areolar complex:** thickening, retraction (new versus longstanding), peau d'orange, nipple inversion, skin calcifications, sinus tracts, scars.

**Parenchyma by quadrant:** masses, architectural distortion, asymmetries, calcifications, ductal changes, trabecular thickening, oedema.

**Axilla and chest wall:** lymph node morphology (size, cortical thickness, hilar fat, shape, level), vessels, muscle involvement, rib lesions.

**Implants (if present):** location (subglandular/subpectoral), peri-implant fluid, capsular calcification, integrity.

**Standardise lesion location and size:**

- Laterality, clockface, distance from nipple (cm), depth (anterior/middle/posterior third).
- Measure in millimetres; give three dimensions for masses. For calcifications, state the maximum span in two planes — this drives surgical planning.
- Distinguish **multifocal** (within a quadrant) from **multicentric** (across quadrants) disease when it is relevant to management.

**Modality-specific descriptors:**

*Mammography / DBT:*
- **Masses:** shape (round / oval / irregular), margin (circumscribed / obscured / microlobulated / indistinct / spiculated), density relative to fibroglandular tissue, associated features (skin or nipple change, trabecular thickening, calcifications).
- **Calcifications:** morphology and distribution. Morphology drives suspicion: fine pleomorphic or fine linear/branching calcifications are high risk; amorphous or coarse heterogeneous are indeterminate; coarse "popcorn," lucent-centred, vascular, and skin calcifications are typically benign. Distribution matters almost as much: segmental or linear distribution implies a ductal origin and raises suspicion for DCIS. State the span.
- **Asymmetries:** classify as focal, global, or developing. A developing asymmetry requires proof of change — do not label it as such if priors are unavailable. Confirm with spot compression or DBT.

*Ultrasound:*
- **Masses:** shape, margin (circumscribed / indistinct / angular / microlobulated / spiculated), orientation (parallel versus non-parallel to skin), echogenicity, posterior features (enhancement / shadowing / none), internal architecture, vascularity.
- **Orientation** encodes important biology: a taller-than-wide (non-parallel) mass is growing across tissue planes — a red flag.
- **Ducts:** dilatation, intraductal lesion, vascular stalk.
- **Nodes:** cortical thickness in millimetres, focal bulge versus diffuse thickening, hilar fat preservation, shape.

*MRI:*
- **Background parenchymal enhancement (BPE):** minimal / mild / moderate / marked. High BPE reduces specificity and should be noted.
- Classify as **mass**, **non-mass enhancement (NME)**, or **focus/foci**.
- **Mass:** shape, margin, internal enhancement pattern (homogeneous / heterogeneous / rim-enhancing), T2 signal, diffusion restriction. Use kinetics as supporting evidence, not the primary decision-driver — morphology takes precedence.
- **NME:** distribution (focal / linear / segmental / regional / diffuse) and internal pattern (homogeneous / heterogeneous / clumped / clustered ring). Segmental clumped or clustered-ring NME is the classic MRI appearance of DCIS and should not be under-called.
- **Implants:** describe intracapsular rupture signs (linguine sign, subcapsular line) and extracapsular silicone separately.

### 6) Impression and category

Write a **single, integrated conclusion**. If there are multiple findings, categorise each, then give an overall M/U category that determines management. Use plain language that mirrors the category: "benign," "indeterminate — biopsy recommended," "highly suspicious of malignancy." Avoid sitting on the fence with language that the category itself does not support.

### 7) Recommendation

State the next step and, where relevant, a timescale:

- Discharge to routine recall (screening) or GP (symptomatic), with safety-netting advice
- Additional views or targeted ultrasound
- Image-guided core biopsy or vacuum-assisted excision (state preferred modality and why)
- Clip placement and post-procedure mammogram
- Staging axillary ultrasound with or without needle sampling
- MRI for extent assessment or occult primary
- MDT discussion

If there is discordance between modalities or between imaging and pathology, name it and propose resolution. Do not leave discordance implicit.

## Structured reporting in screening

### Double reading and arbitration

First and second reads must be concise and codeable. Use NBSS reason codes where available. The standard output is binary — **routine recall** or **recall with reason** — and the reason should be specific and measurable (e.g., "new group of amorphous microcalcifications, left UOQ, 7 cm from nipple, approximately 20 mm span"), not vague (e.g., "possible calcifications").

When readers disagree, the arbitration report should explain the deciding rationale. Record this — it is auditable and a valuable teaching resource.

### Recall assessment reports

- Summarise the screening finding prompting recall, with annotated laterality, quadrant, clockface, and distance from nipple.
- List the targeted assessment imaging performed (DBT, magnification views, ultrasound).
- Describe findings using structured terms (as above), assign M and U categories, and state a clear outcome: assessment benign with discharge; biopsy performed (state category and provisional expectation); technical repeat required (rare — justify explicitly).
- Document biopsy details: guidance, needle gauge, vacuum assistance, clip deployed, specimen radiograph confirming representative sampling of the target.

### Common pitfalls in screening reports

- **Vague recall reasons** — be specific and measurable; "calcifications" is not enough.
- **Failing to localise** — always give clockface, distance from nipple, and approximate extent.
- **No category or action** — a long description without a conclusion is not a report.
- **Not comparing with prior rounds** — label a finding a "developing asymmetry" only when change is proven.

## Structured reporting in symptomatic triple assessment

### Integrating P, M, U, and B

Begin with the symptom and P-score. **A normal mammogram does not dismiss a P4 or P5 clinical finding** — acknowledge the discordance, escalate ultrasound, and proceed to biopsy if clinical suspicion is high. The triple assessment exists precisely because each component can miss cancer.

Assign M and U categories to each relevant finding, provide an overall assessment, and state the plan. After biopsy, append or addend the B category, histological diagnosis, and an explicit concordance statement.

### Documenting concordance

This is one of the most important habits to develop. Write it every time:

- **Concordant benign:** *"U2 oval circumscribed homogeneous hypoechoic mass, 3 o'clock, 4 cm from nipple, consistent with fibroadenoma. Core biopsy B2 fibroadenoma: concordant. Discharge with safety-netting."*
- **Discordant — requires action:** *"M5 spiculated mass right UOQ; core biopsy B2 fat necrosis: discordant. Repeat sampling with vacuum-assisted device or surgical excision recommended. Discuss at MDT."*
- **Concordant malignant:** *"M5/U5 spiculated mass; B5b grade 2 invasive carcinoma: concordant. Clip in situ. Refer to MDT for surgical planning."*

Never assume concordance — write it. If you are uncertain whether histology explains the imaging, flag it for MDT discussion rather than defaulting to discharge.

### Axillary assessment

Include a structured axillary report in any proven or suspected cancer, or where nodes are symptomatic. State: side, level (I–III if relevant), number of morphologically abnormal nodes, cortical thickness in millimetres, focal bulge versus diffuse thickening, hilar fat status. Record if sampling was FNA or core biopsy, the number of passes, and the B or cytology result when available.

## Modality-specific structured elements

### Mammography and tomosynthesis

**Masses:** The key suspicious features are spiculation, indistinct margins, and associated architectural distortion — these reflect desmoplastic response and infiltrative growth. Shape alone is less discriminating: an irregular mass with circumscribed margins may be a rapidly growing fibroadenoma; a round mass with spiculated margins is highly suspicious.

**Calcifications:** Morphology maps to pathophysiology. Fine linear/branching calcifications fill and cast necrotic DCIS ducts; fine pleomorphic calcifications reflect cellular debris within ducts. These warrant M4–M5 and biopsy. Amorphous calcifications are less specific — distribution and clinical context determine whether M3 (biopsy) or M2 (observation) is appropriate. Document the **maximum span** in two planes for any calcifications being biopsied, because extent determines surgical approach.

**Benign patterns to recognise confidently:** Oil cysts with fat density and rim calcification; coarse "popcorn" fibroadenoma calcifications; vascular calcifications; dermal calcifications (confirm with tangential view if needed). Over-calling these wastes assessment resource and generates unnecessary patient anxiety.

**DBT:** Document when used and what it adds. It is particularly valuable for resolving summation artefact that mimics distortion, and for demonstrating true spiculation.

### Ultrasound

**Suspicious features:** Non-parallel (taller-than-wide) orientation, angular or spiculated margins, heterogeneous echotexture, posterior acoustic shadowing, absent compressibility, peripheral or chaotic vascularity.

**Benign features:** Oval parallel mass with circumscribed margins and homogeneous hypoechoic echotexture (fibroadenoma); anechoic simple cyst with posterior enhancement and imperceptible wall; clustered microcysts; intramammary node with fatty hilum and thin cortex (<3 mm).

**Measure three dimensions** and document orientation. Note the echogenic halo when present (desmoplastic response — suspicious). For subareolar lesions, document relationship to ducts. After biopsy, confirm clip–lesion correlation on post-procedure images.

**Never report "no focal abnormality" without specifically interrogating the symptomatic area.** If you could not adequately assess a region, say why and suggest a solution.

### MRI

MRI is a high-sensitivity, lower-specificity tool. Its role in UK practice is defined: extent assessment in known cancer (particularly lobular histology or size discordance), occult primary with axillary metastasis, response monitoring during neoadjuvant chemotherapy, and implant integrity assessment in selected cases.
