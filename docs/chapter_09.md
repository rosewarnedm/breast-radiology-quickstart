## Chapter 9: Correlation with Ultrasound and Biopsy

---

## Why Correlation Matters

The triple assessment pathway — clinical examination, imaging, and tissue diagnosis — only works when its three components are interpreted together rather than in isolation. Correlation is the active process of deciding whether your mammographic finding, your sonographic target, and your histological result are truly the same lesion. This matters in two directions: a suspicious image with a benign biopsy result may represent a false negative unless you can demonstrate that the biopsy actually sampled the imaging abnormality; and an incidental benign lesion biopsied instead of the intended target wastes resource and may delay diagnosis.

Good correlation minimises false negatives, avoids sampling the wrong thing, guides the most appropriate biopsy technique, and gives the MDT the information it needs to act safely. In the screening context this is especially important because you are often working without symptoms and the clinical component of triple assessment contributes little; imaging and pathology must therefore be particularly robust.

## 1. Fundamentals of Image–Pathology Correlation

### 1.1 The Core Question

Every time you receive a histology result for an imaged lesion, ask three questions in sequence:

1. Did we sample the right target?
2. Does the pathology plausibly explain the imaging appearance?
3. Is the level of certainty sufficient to guide management?

If any answer is uncertain, act — repeat imaging, resample, escalate to vacuum-assisted biopsy (VAB), or discuss at MDT. The habit of explicitly asking and documenting these questions is what separates safe from unsafe practice.

### 1.2 What Each Modality Actually Depicts

Understanding why a lesion looks different on mammography and ultrasound helps you predict when they will and will not correlate.

Mammography depicts X-ray attenuation of compressed, overlapping tissue. Masses and calcifications are conspicuous, but projection geometry means a lesion's apparent position shifts between the craniocaudal (CC) and mediolateral oblique (MLO) views, and parenchymal summation can mimic or obscure a real abnormality.

Ultrasound depicts acoustic interfaces in uncompressed tissue. Masses appear as differences in echogenicity and architecture. Critically, microcalcifications are often invisible sonographically unless they are coarse, clustered, or associated with a mass or duct — this is one of the principal reasons that suspicious microcalcifications require stereotactic or digital breast tomosynthesis (DBT)-guided biopsy rather than ultrasound guidance.

Tomosynthesis reduces tissue overlap, localises findings in depth, and often clarifies whether an apparent mammographic asymmetry is real parenchymal tissue or summation.

MRI detects perfusion and T2 signal characteristics and will identify occult cancers missed by other modalities, but access to MRI-guided biopsy is limited in the UK and is reserved for designated centres.

Appreciating these differences explains why the same malignancy may be a subtle architectural distortion on mammography, an ill-defined hypoechoic shadow on ultrasound, and an enhancing irregular mass on MRI — and why "it looks benign on ultrasound" is an insufficient reason to dismiss a suspicious mammographic finding.

## 2. From Mammogram to Ultrasound: A Systematic Approach

### 2.1 Converting Mammographic Location to a Sonographic Search Plan

Before you put the probe on the patient, translate the mammographic finding into a precise search target. Record:

- **Side and clock-face position** (using patient-supine orientation as used for ultrasound)
- **Distance from nipple (DFN)** in centimetres, measured along the mammographic image and adjusted for compression
- **Depth zone** — anterior (subcutaneous/subdermal), middle (fibroglandular), or posterior (retroglandular/prechest wall)
- **Relationship to landmarks**: pectoral muscle edge, visible vessels, surgical scars, skin markers

Carrying the same descriptors through the mammography report, the ultrasound record, and the biopsy note creates an auditable chain of correlation.

### 2.2 Triangulation Between Views

On the MLO projection, medial lesions appear higher and lateral lesions appear lower than their true anatomical position — this is sometimes summarised as the "MULD" mnemonic (Medial Up, Lateral Down). A true lateral view (ML or LM), when available, removes this parallax and gives you a more reliable vertical co-ordinate.

On DBT, slices are numbered from one surface of the compressed breast to the other; know your unit's convention for which end is tube-side (anterior) and which is detector-side (posterior). This depth information translates directly to the anterior, middle, and posterior thirds on ultrasound.

Remember that compression redistributes tissue: a lesion sitting posteriorly on a mammogram may sit more anteriorly in the uncompressed breast during ultrasound. Allow for this when planning your depth of search.

### 2.3 Performing the Targeted Ultrasound

Position the patient to approximate the mammographic geometry — supine with arm abducted, with a slight contralateral roll for lateral lesions and ipsilateral roll for medial ones. Scan in radial and antiradial planes through the predicted sector at the predicted DFN, sweeping through the expected depth zone.

Use light transducer pressure initially. Over-compression can efface subtle hypoechoic distortions and soft masses. Adjust focus and frequency to centre on the zone of interest. If the finding was in or near a duct on mammography, track from the nipple outwards in the affected segment.

If after a careful, methodical search you cannot identify a confident sonographic correlate, this is important diagnostic information — it means the lesion is mammography-only and must be biopsied under the appropriate guidance (stereotactic or DBT), not under ultrasound using a "substitute" target.

## 3. Recognising Ultrasound Correlates of Mammographic Findings

### Circumscribed Mass

Typically corresponds to an oval or round, parallel, circumscribed, hypoechoic or isoechoic mass — often a fibroadenoma or cyst. Check for acoustic enhancement (cyst) or posterior shadowing (calcification, dense fibrosis). A circumscribed mass on mammography is usually benign, but mucinous carcinoma, papillary carcinoma, and some triple-negative cancers can also appear circumscribed — do not assume benignity from shape alone.

### Spiculated Mass

Usually corresponds to an irregular, non-parallel hypoechoic mass with angular margins and posterior acoustic shadowing. The echogenic halo seen on ultrasound corresponds histologically to the desmoplastic reaction responsible for the mammographic spicules. When you see this appearance, the pre-test probability of malignancy is high.

### Architectural Distortion

Look for subtle convergence of Cooper's ligaments towards a central point, focal posterior shadowing without a discrete mass, and a slightly hypoechoic area within the fibroglandular tissue. This can be genuinely difficult to find — invasive lobular carcinoma in particular may produce only subtle tethering.

### Calcifications

Malignant pleomorphic or fine linear microcalcifications are usually invisible on ultrasound. You may see echogenic foci within a hypoechoic duct or mass, but a negative ultrasound does not exclude the calcifications. Do not allow a negative ultrasound to divert you away from stereotactic VAB for suspicious calcifications.

### Focal Asymmetry

Often represents summation artifact. On ultrasound, look for a true mass or architectural change; compare with the contralateral side. If the breast tissue looks symmetrically normal at the mapped location and the mammographic asymmetry effaces on DBT or spot compression, no further action is usually needed. If the asymmetry persists on additional mammographic views but has no ultrasound correlate, biopsy should be stereotactic.

### Common Pitfalls

- **Anisotropy**: angling the beam can make fibrous tissue appear hypoechoic and mimic a lesion; sweep and fan to confirm persistence
- **Accepting a nearby fibroadenoma as the target**: always verify that the DFN and clock-face position of your ultrasound finding genuinely match the mammographic finding before accepting it as a correlate
- **Missing retroareolar and deep upper outer quadrant lesions**: adjust patient position and scan the tail of Spence and subareolar ducts specifically

## 4. Choosing the Biopsy Guidance Modality

The principle is straightforward: **choose the modality that most directly and reliably targets the imaging abnormality**.

| Situation | Preferred guidance |
|---|---|
| Confident US correlate of any mammographic finding | US-guided core biopsy or VAB |
| Suspicious calcifications ± US correlate | Stereotactic/DBT-guided VAB |
| Architectural distortion without US correlate | DBT-guided VAB |
| Mammography-only mass without US correlate | DBT/stereotactic guided VAB |
| MRI-only lesion | MRI-guided biopsy (referral to designated centre) |

Never biopsy an adjacent benign-appearing lesion as a proxy for a suspicious mammographic target. If your ultrasound correlate does not convincingly match the mammographic position, either continue your search or change to the appropriate guidance modality.

## 5. Biopsy Techniques

### 5.1 Ultrasound-Guided Core Biopsy

The workhorse of breast assessment. Use a 14G automated core with a 15–22 mm throw; adjust throw to keep the needle tip away from the chest wall. Infiltrate local anaesthetic subcutaneously and along the needle track; consider hydrodissection to lift a lesion away from the pleura for posterior lesions.

Take three to five cores from a mass, documenting the needle traversing the target in real time. For heterogeneous lesions or when a special type is suspected (papillary, mucinous), consider additional cores to allow immunohistochemistry. Place a titanium marker clip for any lesion that may need surgical localisation, any cancer, and any B3 lesion; document clip position with post-deployment imaging.

### 5.2 Vacuum-Assisted Biopsy (VAB) and Excision (VAE)

VAB uses a rotating cutting aperture and vacuum aspiration to take contiguous larger-volume cores through a single insertion. Use 8–10G for diagnostic VAB, 7–10G for therapeutic excision. Indications include calcifications (under stereotactic/DBT guidance), architectural distortion, small masses requiring larger sampling volume, papillary lesions, and B3 lesions where upgrade assessment or therapeutic excision is planned. After calcification VAB, specimen radiography is essential to confirm retrieval. Place a clip routinely, as complete sampling can remove all visible calcification.

### 5.3 Stereotactic and DBT-Guided VAB

Used for mammography-only targets: calcifications, architectural distortion, and non-calcified masses not visible on ultrasound. DBT guidance is particularly advantageous for architectural distortion because the three-dimensional localisation is more precise than on two-dimensional stereotaxis. Take adequate cores — typically six to twelve or more depending on gauge, distribution, and the extent of calcification. Specimen radiography is mandatory after sampling calcifications. Document residual calcification and clip position.

### 5.4 Practical Considerations

**Anticoagulation**: many centres proceed with ultrasound-guided core biopsy on aspirin alone; management of clopidogrel and direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) varies — follow your local policy and weigh the risk of delay against bleeding risk.

**Consent**: discuss bruising, haematoma, pain, infection, vasovagal events, and the rare risk of pneumothorax when working near the chest wall. Mention the small risk of clip migration.

**Documentation**: always record consent, technique, needle gauge and throw, number of cores, imaging confirming needle placement, clip type and post-deployment position, immediate complications, and your planned correlation pathway.

## 6. The UK Scoring Systems in Context

The P/M/U/B scoring system exists to structure clinical decision-making and to create a common language for MDT discussion — it is not a substitute for judgement.

- **P1–P5**: clinical examination findings (normal to malignant)
- **M1–M5**: mammographic assessment (normal to malignant; M0 = technically inadequate)
- **U1–U5**: ultrasound assessment (normal to malignant)
- **B1–B5**: core biopsy histology

**B1** (normal tissue or inadequate) is not reassuring if the imaging is M3, M4, or M5 — it usually means the target was missed. Repeat sampling, often with a different technique.

**B2** (benign, e.g., fibroadenoma, fibrocystic change) is reassuring only when concordant with a benign imaging phenotype. A B2 result for a spiculated mass is discordant and requires resampling.

**B3** (uncertain malignant potential) includes atypical ductal hyperplasia (ADH), flat epithelial atypia (FEA), lobular neoplasia, radial scar/complex sclerosing lesion, papilloma with or without atypia, and mucocoele-like lesions. Upgrade rates to malignancy vary considerably between subtypes and between centres. Management is MDT-directed and typically involves VAE or diagnostic excision; the NHSBSP provides guidance on B3 lesion management.

**B4** (suspicious but insufficient for definitive diagnosis) should prompt re-biopsy, typically with VAB, or proceed directly to diagnostic excision per MDT decision.

**B5a** (in situ) and **B5b** (invasive malignancy) should trigger receptor status assessment; if the tissue retrieved is insufficient, additional cores may be needed before the MDT meeting.

## 7. Radiology–Pathology Concordance

Concordance assessment is not optional — it must be performed explicitly after every biopsy and documented.

**Concordant examples**:
- Oval circumscribed mass on mammography + U2/U3 + fibroadenoma (B2) → concordant; consider discharge or short-interval follow-up per MDT
- DBT architectural distortion + U3 + radial scar (B3) → concordant; manage upgrade risk with VAE or surgery per local protocol

**Discordant examples requiring action**:
- Spiculated mass (M4/U4) + fibrocystic change (B2) → discordant; resample with VAB or alternative guidance
- Pleomorphic segmental calcifications (M4) + benign secretory calcifications on histology without calcification retrieval on specimen radiograph → discordant; repeat stereotactic VAB and confirm retrieval

When a result is discordant, document this explicitly and discuss at MDT promptly. Do not allow a benign result to close the case if it does not account for the imaging finding. Building a culture where discordance is always named and acted upon is one of the most effective quality-improvement measures in breast radiology.

## 8. Post-Biopsy Marking and Preoperative Localisation

### Clip Placement

Place a marker clip when:
- A lesion may shrink or disappear during neoadjuvant chemotherapy
- The target is mammography-only and may not be visible at surgery
- The lesion is a B3 abnormality that may require later localisation
- Complete removal by VAB may make the target invisible at surgery

Document clip position with a post-deployment image. Clip migration is more likely after VAB of cystic or loose stromal lesions; an early post-procedure mammogram establishes the baseline position.

### Preoperative Localisation Techniques

Ultrasound-guided localisation is used when the clip or residual lesion is visible on ultrasound. For mammography-only targets, use image-guided localisation — traditionally hookwire under stereotactic or DBT guidance, increasingly replaced by magnetic seeds (Magseed) or radar reflectors (SAVI SCOUT) where available. Choice of technique varies between centres; ensure compatibility with MRI and theatre equipment at your trust.

For extensive disease (e.g., large areas of calcification or multifocal distortion), bracketing with multiple markers is used to define surgical margins.

## 9. Lesion-Specific Correlation and Biopsy Decisions

### Spiculated Masses

Pre-test probability of malignancy is high. A US correlate is usually present. Use ultrasound-guided 14G core or VAB; for lesions close to the chest wall, VAB may be preferable as it allows sampling at a greater distance from the pleura. Mark with a clip. If DBT-only visible, use DBT-guided VAB.

### Architectural Distortion

Find a US correlate if possible; if found, VAB is preferred over core because the upgrade rate from radial scar/complex sclerosing lesion to malignancy is significant with core biopsy alone. If there is no correlate, proceed to DBT-guided VAB. Always place a clip.
