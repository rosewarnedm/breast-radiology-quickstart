# Chapter 6: Suspicious Mammographic Features

## Overview and purpose

Recognising suspicious mammographic features is not about memorising lists — it is about understanding *why* certain appearances are worrying, and using that understanding to interpret images systematically, score them appropriately, and drive the right next step. This chapter covers the key suspicious categories within the framework of UK practice: the NHS Breast Screening Programme (NHSBSP) and triple assessment. Throughout, the emphasis is on linking appearances to underlying biology, integrating findings across modalities, and exercising calibrated judgement rather than applying rigid rules.

---

## The UK context: where mammography sits in the pathway

Two distinct clinical contexts govern how you interpret and score a mammogram.

**Screening (NHSBSP):** Women aged 50–70 are invited every three years (some programmes extend to 47–73). Standard two-view mammography — craniocaudal (CC) and mediolateral oblique (MLO) — is double-read with arbitration for discordant scores. Abnormalities trigger recall to an assessment clinic where additional views, tomosynthesis, targeted ultrasound, and biopsy are available. Prior images are essential for comparison.

**Symptomatic pathway (triple assessment):** A clinical presentation — lump, skin change, nipple discharge, pain — drives imaging. Triple assessment integrates:
- Clinical examination → **P1–P5**
- Imaging (mammography ± ultrasound) → **M1–M5** and/or **U1–U5**
- Tissue sampling → **B1–B5**

The imaging score reflects probability of malignancy:
- **M1** Normal
- **M2** Benign
- **M3** Indeterminate / probably benign
- **M4** Suspicious
- **M5** Highly suggestive of malignancy

In symptomatic practice, M3 findings frequently proceed to biopsy rather than surveillance, because a patient with a symptom warrants tissue diagnosis rather than watchful waiting unless there is a confidently benign explanation with MDT agreement.

**Modality roles in brief:**
- *Mammography:* characterises calcifications, masses, distortion, asymmetries, and associated features.
- *Digital breast tomosynthesis (DBT):* reduces tissue overlap; improves detection of architectural distortion and spiculated margins; widely used at UK assessment clinics.
- *Ultrasound:* characterises masses, guides biopsy, evaluates axillary nodes; first-line for symptomatic younger women and for biopsy of sonographically visible targets.
- *MRI:* problem-solving and staging in selected cases; not a general screening tool.
- *Image-guided biopsy:* ultrasound-guided core biopsy for sonographically visible lesions; stereotactic or DBT-guided vacuum-assisted biopsy (VAB) for calcifications and distortion without an ultrasound correlate.

## Anatomy that drives appearance

Understanding normal breast anatomy makes suspicious features legible rather than arbitrary.

The breast is a branching duct–lobular tree set within fibrous stroma and fat. Ducts converge radially toward the nipple; Cooper's ligaments tether the skin to the deep fascia. On mammography, fat is radiolucent and fibroglandular tissue is dense. When malignancy invades, it provokes a **desmoplastic stromal reaction** — fibroblast activation that contracts the surrounding tissue, producing spiculation and retraction. This is the biological basis of the most worrying mammographic signs.

Calcifications arise from several mechanisms:
- **Secretory or involutional change:** typically coarse and benign.
- **Dystrophic calcification:** after trauma, surgery, or fat necrosis.
- **Necrosis within ducts or invasive tumour:** produces fine, angular, fragmented calcifications — the hallmark of DCIS and some invasive cancers.

Architectural distortion occurs when normal radial-lamellar patterning is disrupted — by malignant infiltration, a radial scar, or post-surgical scarring.

Lymphatic obstruction and increased interstitial pressure underlie the trabecular thickening and skin oedema seen in inflammatory carcinoma and lymphovascular-rich cancers.

## How to look: a systematic approach

Good perception requires a deliberate routine, not just pattern recognition.

**On every mammogram, check:** skin and nipple; retroglandular fat strip; subareolar region; axillary tail; far medial breast; and posterior tissue depth. Compare both breasts and, critically, compare with prior studies — interval change often distinguishes significance.

**Triangulate lesion location:** compare position on CC and MLO to determine depth (anterior, middle, posterior third) and quadrant. This directly guides targeted ultrasound.

**Use supplementary views purposefully:**
- *Spot compression:* resolves summation artefact; better defines margins.
- *Magnification views:* essential for characterising microcalcification morphology and confirming layering.
- *True lateral (90°):* demonstrates dependent layering of milk of calcium.
- *DBT:* dissects tissue overlap; superior for distortion and spiculation.

**Recognise technical pitfalls:** motion blur softens margins and calcification morphology — repeat before under-calling. Suboptimal positioning hides posterior and medial lesions. Skin lesions, talc, and deodorant project as apparent calcifications; mark with a skin marker (BB) to confirm dermal location.

## Suspicious mammographic categories

### 1) Masses

A mass is a space-occupying lesion visible on two projections. The features that determine its M-score relate to shape, margins, and density — all of which reflect the underlying biological behaviour.

**Shape:**
- Irregular or angular shapes suggest infiltration. Round or oval shapes favour benignity, but are not definitive — some triple-negative cancers and metastases appear circumscribed.

**Margins** — the most diagnostically important feature:
- *Spiculated:* thin straight lines radiating from the lesion. Reflects desmoplasia tethering Cooper's ligaments. Highly predictive of malignancy → almost always **M5** unless a clear benign explanation exists (e.g. a known recent surgical scar at precisely that site).
- *Indistinct/obscured:* raises concern, particularly if persistent after spot compression or on DBT.
- *Microlobulated:* fine, numerous undulations can be suspicious.
- *Circumscribed:* favours benignity, but does not exclude malignancy — always correlate with ultrasound.

**Density:** A mass denser than adjacent fibroglandular tissue is more suspicious. Fat-containing lesions (oil cysts, hamartomas, intramammary nodes) are almost always benign.

**Associated features** that elevate suspicion: architectural distortion, skin thickening or retraction, nipple retraction, trabecular thickening or oedema, suspicious calcifications, abnormal axillary nodes.

**Key differentials:**

| Feature | Fibroadenoma | Carcinoma |
|---|---|---|
| Shape | Oval, gently macrolobulated | Irregular |
| Margins | Circumscribed | Spiculated, indistinct |
| Density | Iso- or low-density | Often high-density |
| Calcification | Coarse "popcorn" if present | Fine pleomorphic or linear |
| Ultrasound | Parallel, homogeneous, gentle lobulations | Non-parallel, angular, posterior shadow |

Fat necrosis may produce a spiculated mass without internal fat lucency, especially in early evolution — when in doubt, biopsy rather than reassure. An intramammary lymph node is recognised by its oval shape, circumscribed margins, upper outer quadrant location, and **lucent fatty hilum**; loss of the hilum or eccentric cortical thickening warrants biopsy.

**Scoring guidance:** Spiculation → M5. Irregular, high-density, ill-defined mass with distortion → M5. New or enlarging ill-defined mass without classic morphology → M4 pending DBT/ultrasound.

**Ultrasound correlation:** Spiculated masses correlate with infiltrative histologies (invasive carcinoma of no special type, tubular carcinoma) and typically show angular margins, posterior shadowing, and non-parallel orientation. Mucinous carcinoma may appear deceptively circumscribed with posterior enhancement — do not dismiss a circumscribed mass on ultrasound without considering the clinical context and patient age.

### 2) Microcalcifications

Calcification pattern — morphology plus distribution — is the primary route to identifying DCIS and some invasive cancers. The biology is straightforward: high-grade DCIS produces central necrosis that calcifies; low-grade DCIS and adenosis produce secretory calcifications of different character.

**Suspicious morphological patterns:**
- *Fine linear / fine linear branching ("casting"):* calcified necrotic debris filling and casting ducts. Highly suspicious for high-grade DCIS → **M5**.
- *Fine pleomorphic:* variable shapes and sizes, typically tiny and angular → **M4–M5** depending on distribution and extent.
- *Amorphous / indistinct:* hazy, lacking morphological detail. Can represent low-grade DCIS or benign secretory change. Context and distribution determine scoring — a new tight cluster in a postmenopausal woman → M4; stable bilateral diffuse → M3 or M2.
- *Coarse heterogeneous:* larger than pleomorphic; intermediate suspicion when clustered.

**Benign patterns to confidently recognise:**
- *Skin calcifications:* lucent-centred, punctate, at the skin surface; confirm with BB marker.
- *Vascular:* parallel "tram-track" calcification following vessel walls.
- *Milk of calcium:* dependent layering — crescentic appearance on the true lateral, amorphous on CC. Benign cystic change.
- *Secretory / plasma cell mastitis:* coarse rod-like or "tea-cup" calcifications radiating subareolarly, often bilateral. Benign.
- *Dystrophic:* coarse, large, irregular; post-trauma, post-surgery, or post-radiotherapy.

**Distribution modifies the significance of morphology:**
- *Segmental or linear:* tracking along a ductal system toward the nipple — strongly suspicious for DCIS, regardless of morphology.
- *Clustered / grouped:* suspicious if tight and with concerning morphology.
- *Regional:* suspicious with worrying morphology; diffuse bilateral distribution usually benign.

**Practical work-up:** Obtain magnification CC and true lateral views. Confirm whether calcifications layer (milk of calcium) or remain fixed (potential DCIS). Compare with prior studies — new clusters are more concerning than stable ones, but stability over 2–3 years does not completely exclude slow-growing low-grade DCIS.

**Biopsy:** Stereotactic or DBT-guided VAB is standard. Obtain specimen radiographs to confirm calcification retrieval. Deploy a marker clip and obtain post-procedure mammography to document clip position relative to residual disease.

**Radiology–pathology concordance:** Fine linear/branching calcifications → expect high-grade DCIS (B5a). Amorphous calcifications → low-grade DCIS, sclerosing adenosis, or apocrine change are all plausible; if pathology returns B2 benign for an M4/M5 target, this is discordant — discuss in MDT and consider repeat biopsy.

### 3) Architectural distortion (AD)

**Definition:** Focal disruption of the normal parenchymal architecture — straight lines or spicules radiating from a point, or focal retraction — without a discrete mass visible. This is one of the most commonly missed mammographic abnormalities, particularly on standard 2D mammography.

**Why it matters:** AD on mammography has two main causes that are radiologically indistinguishable — **invasive carcinoma** (or DCIS with stromal reaction) and **radial scar / complex sclerosing lesion (CSL)**. Both require histological assessment.

**DBT is transformative here:** by removing overlapping tissue, it reveals spicules that are invisible or ambiguous on 2D. In assessment clinics, DBT should be used routinely when AD is suspected.

**Scoring:** Isolated AD without a benign explanation is at least **M4**. AD with associated suspicious calcifications, or with a suspicious ultrasound correlate, is **M5**.

**Key differential features:**
- *Post-surgical scar:* tethering and fat lucency at a known operative site; stabilises or regresses over time; compare carefully with priors.
- *Radial scar / CSL:* characteristic "black star" on magnification — central radiolucency with long, thin spicules. Cannot reliably be distinguished from malignancy without biopsy.
- *Fat necrosis:* distortion with internal fat density or evolving rim calcification.
- *Focal fibrosis / sclerosing adenosis:* may cause subtle distortion; biopsy clarifies.

**Biopsy route:** If no ultrasound correlate, DBT-guided VAB is appropriate. If ultrasound identifies a lesion, ultrasound-guided core biopsy is preferred. B3 results (radial scar, atypical ductal hyperplasia) from AD require MDT discussion; many UK centres perform vacuum-assisted excision rather than routine surgical excision for radial scars, though practice varies.

### 4) Asymmetries

Asymmetry is unilateral increased tissue density that does not fulfil the criteria for a mass. It is a spectrum, and the degree of concern depends on type and behaviour.

**Types:**
- *One-view asymmetry:* seen on only one projection — most often summation artefact. Obtain spot compression and an orthogonal view. If it persists on two views, manage as focal asymmetry.
- *Focal asymmetry:* seen on two views but without defined margins. If new or increasing, warrants spot compression, DBT, and targeted ultrasound.
- *Developing asymmetry:* new or enlarging compared with prior studies. This is the most clinically significant type — treat with heightened suspicion → M4 if persistent after assessment, unless there is a confidently benign explanation.
- *Global asymmetry:* involves most of a breast; usually a normal variant or post-surgical change. Malignancy is uncommon unless accompanied by skin thickening, nipple change, or suspicious calcifications.

**Correlation:** Ultrasound targeted to the area of asymmetry may reveal a discrete mass or focal shadowing. If ultrasound is negative but concern persists, DBT assessment is valuable; MRI may be appropriate in selected cases (e.g. strong family history, discordance).

**Pitfall:** Positioning differences between studies create pseudo-asymmetry. Compare like-for-like; repeat views if positioning is clearly non-comparable.

### 5) Ductal and subareolar changes

- A solitary dilated duct containing an internal mass or calcifications may represent a papilloma or DCIS. Suspicious when accompanied by segmental calcifications or clinically relevant nipple discharge (bloody, spontaneous, unilateral, single-duct).
- Fine linear calcifications extending in a segmental distribution toward the nipple indicate high risk for DCIS and merit M5 scoring.
- Ultrasound is valuable for detecting intraductal lesions; galactography is rarely used in contemporary UK practice.
- Subareolar distortion or a new subareolar mass should not be dismissed; assess carefully with spot compression, DBT, and ultrasound, and note any clinical nipple retraction.

---

### 6) Skin, nipple, trabecular changes, and oedema

These are associated features that, in combination with a primary lesion, elevate the M-score.

- **Skin thickening:** Localised thickening tethered to a spiculated mass is suspicious. Generalised unilateral thickening with trabecular prominence raises the possibility of inflammatory carcinoma but also occurs with benign oedema (cardiac failure, axillary node obstruction) — clinical correlation is essential.
- **Nipple retraction:** New retraction is concerning for subareolar malignancy. Long-standing bilateral inversion without change is usually a normal variant.
- **Trabecular thickening and oedema:** Non-specific in isolation; become meaningful when focal and associated with a suspicious mass or distortion.

---

### 7) Lymph nodes

Normal intramammary and axillary nodes are oval, circumscribed, with a radiolucent fatty hilum and a thin, uniform cortex.

**Suspicious features:** Round shape; cortical thickening — especially eccentric or focal bulge; loss of the fatty hilum; microcalcifications (may indicate metastasis or silicone deposits).

Correlate suspicious nodes with ultrasound, which allows direct cortical thickness measurement. Biopsy (ultrasound-guided core or fine needle aspiration) should be performed if the node is suspicious in a patient with confirmed or suspected ipsilateral breast malignancy, as this directly influences surgical planning.
